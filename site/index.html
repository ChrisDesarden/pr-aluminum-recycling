Propuesta de trabajo · 2026 · v1.0

Cierra el ciclo de la lata.15 000 t/año de aluminio reciclado para Puerto Rico.

Cada año, cerca de abandonan la isla como fardos de chatarra, navegan bajo la Ley Jones, se funden en Texas o Mississippi y regresan como latas nuevas. Proponemos cerrar ese ciclo en la isla: una planta secundaria de aluminio en terrenos de PRIDCO, alimentada por un sistema de depósito-reembolso y una red de puntos de entrega de costa a costa.

In plain English

We want to build a factory that turns empty cans back into aluminum without shipping them to the mainland.

↻

Right now, most Puerto Ricans throw away aluminum cans. Those cans get baled, shipped to the U.S. mainland, melted down, and the new aluminum is shipped back as cans. This proposal is a plan to keep that whole loop on the island instead: collect the cans locally, melt them locally, and sell the recycled aluminum to can-makers. It would save a lot of energy, create local jobs, and keep money from leaking off the island.

La versión de un párrafo

Qué significa esto

Es un plan de negocios para una fábrica de reciclaje del tamaño de una isla.

↻

El plan es construir una sola planta mediana que procese 15 000 toneladas de latas usadas al año. Para obtener suficientes latas, la isla añadiría un depósito de 5¢ por cada lata de aluminio vendida: pagas un níquel al comprar la bebida y lo recuperas al devolver la lata vacía. La planta se ubicaría en terreno industrial cerca de Ponce o Guayama, emplearía cerca de 62 personas directamente y se financiaría principalmente con un bono respaldado por esos depósitos más créditos fiscales federales de energía limpia. Las proyecciones indican recuperación de la inversión en unos tres años y una sólida rentabilidad a largo plazo.

Construir una sola planta secundaria de fundición de aluminio y lingote en una parcela industrial de PRIDCO en Guayama o Ponce, licenciada bajo estándares de IBHS y un permiso menor de aire NSR de la JCA. Alimentarla con un sistema isleño de depósito-reembolso de 5¢ sobre latas de aluminio, más una red de puntos de entrega en supermercados, centros comerciales y espacios cívicos que cubra los 78 municipios. Vender T-bar sow y lingote P1020A a socios compradores (Coca-Cola, Ball, Novelis, Crown) mediante contratos de offtake plurianuales. Usar el y el crédito de producción de la Ley IRA para reducir el costo medio de capital a ~4,5 %.

The proposal is intentionally medium-scaled: large enough to be economically meaningful and to capture a meaningful share of the island's UBC, but small enough that a single site won't concentrate hurricane risk and that permitting is tractable inside one legislative cycle.

¿Por qué Puerto Rico, y por qué ahora?

¿Por qué Puerto Rico?

PR desecha casi todas las latas de aluminio que usa y luego paga por importar latas nuevas.

↻

Puerto Rico usa aproximadamente 17 000 toneladas de latas de aluminio al año, pero solo alrededor del 2 % se recicla aquí. El resto se envía a fundidores del continente bajo la Ley Jones, lo que encarece el transporte. Un depósito de 5¢ por lata —similar a las leyes de envases— daría a la gente una razón para devolver las vacías en lugar de tirarlas. La fábrica se construiría para resistir huracanes y cortes de energía, porque el clima y la red de la isla son desafíos reales. En resumen: el problema es grande, la solución está probada en otros lugares y el momento es oportuno porque existen créditos fiscales federales.

La tasa de reciclaje de la isla es una de las más bajas de EE. UU. y el Caribe, y la economía de la Ley Jones hace que enviar latas fuera de la isla sea particularmente cara. Cerrar el ciclo es técnicamente posible, financieramente viable a escala y atrasado.

El problema

PR genera unas (CIA World Factbook × 33 % de participación de latas; cruzado con cifras per cápita de Abralatas y la entrada de la MRF EcoEléctrica). De eso, solo se recupera a nivel doméstico; el resto se farda y se envía bajo la Ley Jones a fundidores del continente.

La oportunidad

Un esquema de depósito-reembolso de 5¢ —modelado en NSW Australia, Michigan y Connecticut— elevaría la recuperación a , al nivel de Alemania y Bélgica. Sumar un ahorro energético del 95 % vs. la producción primaria, y el caso climático es independiente del caso financiero.

La restricción

La red de PR es frágil (María, Fiona, tarifa de emergencia de LUMA 2025), la electricidad industrial cuesta y la isla está en zona de huracán Cat 5. Cualquier planta a escala isleña debe ser FORTIFIED, con generación propia y asegurada a niveles que las plantas del continente no necesitan.

Advertencias honestas

  • PR no tiene hoy una fundición de aluminio. La fuerza laboral de ingeniería debe reclutarse o capacitarse. Suponemos una rampa de 12 meses para contrataciones directas y 3 años de desarrollo para roles supervisores.
  • Las latas se importan ya fabricadas (no hay planta de laminación de hoja de lata en la isla). Cerrar el ciclo sobre las latas mismas está fuera del alcance; esta propuesta cubre UBC → lingote/sow, que es la etapa de mayor valor de la cadena de reciclaje.
  • Los ingresos por créditos de carbono son elegibles metodológicamente (VCS VM0017/VM0039) pero con volumen limitado en mercados voluntarios. Modelamos $15–25/tCO₂e, no las cifras especulativas de $100+ que han circulado.

Cómo lo hacen otras jurisdicciones

Qué podemos aprender de otros lugares

Otros países ya descubrieron cómo recuperar latas a altas tasas.

↻

Brasil, Japón, la UE, Australia, Islandia y partes de EE. UU. muestran distintas formas de recuperar latas. El patrón común es simple: dar a la gente una razón clara para devolver las vacías (un depósito o una recompensa pagada por productores) y asegurar una fábrica local que las funda. Australia y muchos países europeos usan depósitos. Japón y Brasil confían más en la responsabilidad extendida del productor. La lección para Puerto Rico es que los depósitos funcionan rápido y que un refusor local es la pieza faltante que retiene valor en la isla.

Seis jurisdicciones, seis herramientas distintas. Ninguna es plantilla perfecta para PR, pero juntas dibujan el espacio de diseño.

97.3% recovery (2024) ~417,700 t/yr No cash deposit

1. How the system is structured and governed

Brazil runs the world’s highest-performing aluminum-can recovery system without a consumer deposit. The legal foundation is the National Solid Waste Policy (PNRS, Law 12.305/2010) and its implementing decree, which require producers, importers, distributors, and retailers of disposable packaging to finance and operate logística reversa (reverse-logistics) systems.

For aluminum cans, the sector signs a federal Commitment Agreement administered by Abralatas (can manufacturers) and Abal (aluminum producers). Producers pay into a stewardship pool that guarantees purchase of 100% of the UBC scrap generated. Because aluminum scrap has a high commodity value, the system is largely self-financing: the sale of clean UBC covers collection and transport, with producer fees filling any gaps.

A distinctive governance feature is the formal integration of catadores — independent waste pickers and cooperatives — as recognized service providers. The PNRS explicitly includes them, and the Abralatas/Recicla Latas model channels revenue to cooperative collection points rather than relying solely on municipal contracts.

2. Collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure

  • Decentralized collection: a nationwide web of cooperatives, voluntary drop-off points (PEVs), scrap merchants, and municipal programs. Only ~60% of Brazilian municipalities offer recycling collection, yet the can recovery rate is >95% because the informal/cooperative layer fills the gap.
  • Regional recycling hubs: the largest is Pindamonhangaba, São Paulo, a historic can-sheet and recycling cluster with capacity of roughly 250 t/day. Novelis and Aleris Latasa operate major re-melters and can-sheet plants.
  • Speed: the average can is collected, recycled, and back on shelves in about 30 days.
  • Automation: collection is mostly manual or semi-mechanized; sorting relies on eddy-current and manual separation at MRFs and cooperative facilities.

3. Recovery rates and economic outcomes

In 2024 Brazil recycled 97.3% of aluminum beverage cans sold — the 15th consecutive year above 95%, amounting to 33.9 billion cans (~417,700 t). The rate slipped from 100% in 2022–2023 mainly because a 64% surge in aluminum scrap exports pulled material away from domestic recyclers.

Economic returns flow through the high scrap value. A tonne of clean UBC is worth substantially more than an equivalent mass of mixed recyclables, so cooperatives prioritize cans. Over the past decade the program claims to have avoided 18 Mt CO₂e and 17 Mt of bauxite extraction. No transparent public cost-per-tonne for the can-specific collection stream was found; the system’s surplus comes from scrap resale rather than from public appropriations.

4. How it fits Brazil’s context

Brazil is a continental country with uneven municipal waste services, a large informal waste-picker workforce, and strong domestic can-sheet and re-melting capacity (Novelis, Aleris). The model works because:

  • the metal is valuable enough to pay for its own collection;
  • cooperatives provide low-cost, geographically distributed labor; and
  • domestic recyclers are hungry for feedstock, so scrap is purchased quickly.

The 2024 export surge is a warning: even a mature closed loop can leak material if foreign buyers pay more than domestic recyclers.

5. Lessons for Puerto Rico

  • Producer stewardship + accessible drop-off beats deposit-only. A producer-funded system can reach 95%+ without a 10¢ consumer deposit if the economics are structured so that collection agents are paid immediately for clean UBC.
  • Formalize waste-pickers and community groups as collection partners. PR has active community organizations and informal can collectors; giving them a recognized role and a direct revenue share is faster than building a municipal fleet from scratch.
  • Protect domestic feedstock. Any PR law should require UBC to be offered first to the island plant before export, mirroring the EU’s new Waste Shipment Regulation “right of refusal” logic.
  • Build the re-melter before the rate peaks. Brazil’s loop is tight because re-melters and can-sheet plants exist. PR needs the secondary plant to exist so that collected cans have a buyer.
99.8% collection · 75.7% can-to-can (2024) ~2.09 B cans/yr EPR fee, no cash deposit

1. How the system is structured and governed

Japan’s system is built on the Containers and Packaging Recycling Act (Law No. 112 of 1995, effective 1997). It is an Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) law, not a deposit-return scheme. Consumers sort and municipalities collect; beverage brand owners and packaging producers pay recycling fees to the government-designated Japan Container and Package Recycling Association (JCPRA), which runs public tenders to select recyclers in each prefecture.

The Japan Aluminum Can Recycling Association (JACRA) coordinates industry awareness and publishes the annual recovery and can-to-can rates. Fees are published per fiscal year by material category; for aluminum cans they have historically been around ¥70–100/kg, paid by producers into JCPRA. Municipalities receive support for collection and transport, while recyclers buy the sorted UBC from municipal yards.

2. Collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure

  • Kerbside source separation is dominant. Households put aluminum cans into dedicated transparent bags; collection occurs on scheduled days separate from combustibles and PET. Public recycling stations sit in almost every neighborhood.
  • Vending-machine compression is common in dense urban areas: many machines accept the empty can at the side of the unit, compacting it and improving logistics.
  • Domestic re-melters: Showa Denko (now Resonac), Daiki Aluminium, and Nippon Light Metal consume UBC and produce can-body sheet and other secondary alloys.

3. Recovery rates and economic outcomes

In FY2023 Japan collected 97.5% of aluminum cans; in 2024 JACRA reported 99.8% collection and 75.7% can-to-can — meaning roughly three-quarters of recovered cans returned to new can production, with the remainder downcycled into other aluminum products (still in-country). Demand held steady at about 2.09 billion cans in 2025 as population decline and PET substitution offset each other.

The fee-for-service model means producers bear the system cost, not consumers. This reduces “return friction” — residents do not need to visit a redemption center — but it requires a disciplined, high-compliance sorting culture and a capable municipal collection fleet.

4. How it fits Japan’s context

Japan has high population density, strong local government capacity, a culture of household source separation, and domestic can-sheet and secondary smelting industries. The EPR model fits because municipalities can enforce sorting rules and producers are organized enough to collect and remit fees. It would be harder to replicate in a jurisdiction with weak municipal waste services and no domestic re-melter.

5. Lessons for Puerto Rico

  • Separate “collection rate” from “closed-loop rate.” A 99% collection rate is impressive, but the economically meaningful number for a domestic plant is the share that stays on-island. PR should target both metrics explicitly.
  • EPR fees can substitute for deposits if collection discipline is high. If PR does not pass a 5¢ DRS, a producer-fee system modeled on JCPRA could still work — but only if municipalities deliver clean, sorted UBC to a central processor.
  • Transparent public fee schedules build trust. JCPRA’s annual per-material fee schedule is published; a PR PRO should do the same.
  • Convenience is the key driver. PR should place return points where people already shop — supermarkets, gas stations, pharmacies — rather than expecting dedicated trips.
76.3% average (2023); Germany 99% ~1.2 Mt/yr (Europe aggregate) DRS in 12+ member states

1. How the system is structured and governed

Europe is a patchwork moving toward harmonized high recovery. The Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR, Regulation (EU) 2025/40), in force since February 2025, replaced the 1994 Directive. It mandates that member states hit 80% separate collection of beverage cans by 2029 and 90% by 2033, and it effectively requires deposit-return schemes in most member states.

Two other laws shape aluminum specifically:

  • Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA, 2024/1252): aluminum is listed as a strategic raw material; the EU aims for ≥25% of annual consumption to come from EU recycling by 2030, with stricter processing benchmarks for aluminum.
  • Waste Shipment Regulation recast (2024/1157): from 21 May 2026, member states must notify and justify exports of “green-listed” scrap, including non-hazardous aluminum UBC, giving domestic recyclers a right of refusal before material leaves the EU.

Governance is split: Brussels sets targets, member states design DRS and PRO structures, and industry associations (European Aluminium, Metal Packaging Europe) report aggregate rates and lobby for export curbs.

2. Collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure

  • Mandatory DRS in 12+ member states by 2025 (Germany, Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Netherlands, Belgium, Estonia, Lithuania, Latvia, Ireland, Malta, Slovakia). Deposits are typically €0.10–0.25 per container, with dense Reverse Vending Machine (RVM) networks in every supermarket.
  • Kerbside and bring-bank systems persist in non-DRS states (France, Italy, Spain), yielding lower and more variable recovery.
  • Can-sheet capacity: Germany’s Novelis Nachterstedt and Speira Voerde plants, plus Novelis Göttingen and Pieve (Italy), Hydro (Norway/Germany), and Constellium (France), consume UBC for can-sheet production.

3. Recovery rates and economic outcomes

Europe reached a record 76.3% aluminum beverage can recycling rate in 2023. Mature DRS states cluster at 90–99%; Germany leads at 99%. New DRS launches have produced rapid step changes: Malta, Latvia, and Slovakia jumped from ~50% to >80% within 12–18 months of launch.

The economic driver is a combination of unredeemed-deposit (“breakage”) revenue and producer fees, which fund RVM networks and logistics. EU scrap prices in 2024 ranged roughly €1,400–1,800/t for clean UBC, linked to LME. The new export rules are expected to deepen the domestic closed loop by keeping material in-region.

4. How it fits Europe’s context

Europe has high population density, integrated supply chains, and existing can-sheet capacity. The political consensus for circular-economy regulation is strong, and the single market makes cross-border scrap movements easy to monitor. The EU’s export curbs are a direct response to Asian demand pulling material out of the European loop.

5. Lessons for Puerto Rico

  • Set binding recovery targets and ratchet them. The PPWR’s 80% by 2029 / 90% by 2033 framework gives investors certainty. PR could legislate 60% by 2030, 75% by 2033, and 85% by 2035.
  • Pair a DRS with a “right of refusal” for domestic industry. A PR law should require exporters to offer UBC to the island plant first, at market terms, before shipping scrap off-island.
  • DRS is the fastest lever. Europe’s new DRS states moved faster than its EPR-only states. A 5¢ DRS in PR is the most tested path to 75%+ recovery.
  • Recycled-content mandates create pull. The PPWR’s recycled-content targets give domestic recyclers a guaranteed buyer. PR could require that a rising share of cans sold on-island use post-consumer aluminum.

1. How the system is structured and governed

The United States has no federal packaging EPR or deposit law. Recycling is mostly funded by municipalities through general taxes, with revenue from commodity sales covering a declining share of costs. The result is a patchwork: ten states operate Container Deposit Laws (CDLs or “bottle bills”), while the rest rely on curbside single-stream, which recovers cans at much lower purity and rates.

The ten deposit states are California (CRV), Connecticut, Hawaii, Iowa, Maine, Massachusetts, Michigan, New York, Oregon, and Vermont. Deposits range from 5¢ to 10¢. Michigan and Oregon are at 10¢; California uses 5¢ for containers under 24 oz and 10¢ above. Industry groups — the Aluminum Association and Can Manufacturers Institute (CMI) — publish an annual recycling-rate report and a roadmap targeting 70% by 2030, 80% by 2040, and 90% by 2050.

Packaging EPR is now advancing separately: Maine, Oregon, California, Colorado, Minnesota, Maryland, and Washington passed EPR/PRO laws in 2021–2024, with fees rolling out between 2027 and 2031. Aluminum cans will increasingly be covered by PRO fees, but deposit states still deliver the highest recovery.

2. Collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure

  • Curbside single-stream dominates in >70% of municipalities. MRFs use eddy-current separators and hand sorting, but contamination and breakage are high, and aluminum revenue is often used to subsidize lower-value materials.
  • Redemption centers and RVMs in deposit states capture the bulk of in-state UBC at higher purity and lower contamination.
  • Major domestic can-sheet capacity: Novelis (multiple plants), Arconic, JW Aluminum, and Constellium Muscle Shoals. The U.S. can remelt most of its UBC domestically, but a large share leaks abroad as scrap.

3. Recovery rates and economic outcomes

The U.S. aluminum can recycling rate fell to 43% in 2023, down from a long-term average near 52% since 1990 — the lowest in decades. Of roughly 100 billion cans placed on market (~1.7 Mt of aluminum), the recycled share was about 730,000 t of UBC. More than 61 billion cans were landfilled, representing nearly $1.2 billion worth of aluminum.

The divergence between deposit and non-deposit states is stark: deposit states consistently recover 60–90% of containers, while non-deposit states often fall below 30%. The Aluminum Association and CMI explicitly identify well-designed deposit-return systems as the most effective strategy to reverse the decline.

4. How it fits the U.S. context

The U.S. has large, integrated aluminum and beverage industries, extensive MRF infrastructure, and strong domestic can-sheet capacity. The failure is policy, not technology: a federal system has been blocked by retail and beverage lobbying, leaving states to act alone. The result is that high-value aluminum is treated like a low-grade commodity in most of the country.

5. Lessons for Puerto Rico

  • A state/territory-level DRS is the most-tested U.S. tool. Michigan’s 10¢ deposit and Oregon’s modernized 10¢ scheme show that deposits work when the value is high enough and redemption is convenient.
  • Single-stream alone is not enough. The U.S. decline proves that relying on curbside MRFs to recover cans fails at scale. PR should treat single-stream as a fallback, not the primary can-recovery channel.
  • Protect against export leakage. The U.S. exports ~30–40% of UBC and mixed aluminum scrap. PR should legislate a domestic-first offer before export, so the island plant receives the material.
  • Use PRO fees to backstop the DRS. Emerging U.S. EPR/PRO laws can fund education, RVM siting, and processing subsidies without replacing the deposit itself.
75–80% national (2024) ~3 B cans/yr 10¢ state-level CDS

1. How the system is structured and governed

Australia is the first continent fully covered by Container Deposit Schemes (CDS), after Tasmania launched Recycle Rewards on 1 May 2025. Each state and territory operates its own scheme; there is no federal harmonization. Deposits are uniformly 10¢ per eligible container, funded by beverage producers and importers as a “first seller” fee, with consumers receiving the refund on return.

Key launch dates: South Australia (1977, pioneer), Northern Territory (2012), New South Wales (2017, “Return and Earn” run by TOMRA), ACT (2018), Queensland (2018, “Containers for Change”), Western Australia (2020), Victoria (2023, “CDS Vic”), and Tasmania (2025). Each scheme has a network operator or coordinator appointed by the state government.

The schemes are backed by national packaging policy under the Recycling and Waste Reduction Act 2020 and the Australian Packaging Covenant, which sets mandatory targets and reporting.

2. Collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure

  • RVM-dense networks: TOMRA is the dominant operator in NSW, QLD, and other states; Visy and Envirobank operate in Victoria. NSW alone has more than 600 return points (RVMs + depots).
  • Over-the-counter returns at supermarkets, bottle shops, and community depots provide options for consumers who cannot use machines.
  • Collection logistics: returned containers are counted, compacted, baled, and sent to recyclers. Because the schemes divert the high-value fraction out of kerbside bins, MRFs lose some revenue — a known transition cost.

3. Recovery rates and economic outcomes

National aluminum can recovery climbed from ~67% before CDS rollout to roughly 75–80% by 2024. Mature schemes such as South Australia and the Northern Territory exceed 80%; NSW sits around 70–75%. Victoria’s CDS Vic created an estimated 597 direct jobs and reached >90% public awareness in its first eight months.

The economics rely on unredeemed deposits (“breakage”), which typically run 10–20% of containers and fund network operations. Producer fees net of breakage are roughly AUD$0.03–0.08 per can. Scheme operators report that a 10¢ deposit is high enough to motivate behavior but low enough to keep beverage prices stable.

4. How it fits Australia’s context

Australia is a wealthy, urbanized, island continent with strong state governments and sparse rural populations. The state-by-state approach allowed early adopters (SA, NSW) to prove the model before laggards joined. However, Australia has no major can-sheet rolling mill; most recovered UBC is exported to Korea, Thailand, India, or Indonesia for smelting. The loop is therefore broken at the re-melting/rolling stage.

5. Lessons for Puerto Rico

  • This is the most directly applicable model for an island. A 5¢ DRS with supermarket, mall, and civic drop-off points can lift recovery from <2% to 70%+ within a few years, just as Australia moved from 67% to 75–80%.
  • Keep the deposit lower than 10¢ to start. PR’s per-capita income and can volume argue for a 5¢ deposit initially; it can be raised after the network is dense.
  • Pair the DRS with a domestic re-melter. Australia shows that high recovery without local reprocessing still sends value offshore. PR’s plant is the missing second half of the Australian model.
  • Publish transparent tonnage and redemption data. Australian state schemes publish annual reports on return rates, jobs, and litter reduction, which builds political support.
~88% can redemption; closed loop limited Population ~380,000 Deposit since 1989

1. How the system is structured and governed

Iceland is an outlier: a small, cold-climate island that is simultaneously a global primary-aluminum powerhouse and a pioneer of national deposit-return. Endurvinnslan hf. was founded by law in 1989 (Law No. 52/1989, Regulation 368/2000) and is the only company authorized to pay deposits on bottles and cans. Iceland was one of the first countries in the world to implement a national DRS.

The system is governed by the Ministry of Environment and the Icelandic Recycling Fund (Úrvinnslusjóður), which executes extended producer responsibility for packaging, electronics, batteries, and vehicles. Beverage producers and importers pay fees into the fund, and the fund negotiates processing contracts with waste-management companies.

The deposit is currently a uniform 16 ISK (~US$0.12) per container, with a 3 ISK handling fee. Unredeemed deposits are kept by Endurvinnslan to refinance the system.

2. Collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure

  • 60 return facilities nationwide, including 10 automated locations where consumers do not need to sort or count containers manually.
  • Municipal recycling centers in every town accept aluminum cans in dedicated bins, often compacted on-site.
  • Geographic density: despite a sparse population, Iceland’s ~380,000 residents are concentrated in the Greater Reykjavík area and a handful of coastal towns, making a single national operator feasible.

3. Recovery rates and economic outcomes

Endurvinnslan reports a historical drink-container recycling rate of approximately 85%, with aluminum cans near 88% in recent years and around 91% in 2021. Redemption is not the same as closed-loop can-to-can recycling: Iceland has no domestic can-sheet or can-making capacity. The collected UBC is therefore baled and shipped abroad for re-melting and re-rolling; the metal may return to the island as new can sheet, but that second leg is produced in Europe or Asia, not Iceland. In other words, Iceland redeems cans very well, yet its beverage-can loop remains open at the rolling stage.

The primary-aluminum industry is large — three smelters produce roughly 844,000 t/yr of P1020 ingot — but this is smelting, not can-sheet rolling, and it does not consume UBC. The distinction matters: a jurisdiction can be a world-class primary-aluminum producer and still not close the loop on its own used beverage cans.

4. How it fits Iceland’s context

Iceland’s geology and hydropower made it a primary-aluminum smelting hub (Rio Tinto Alcan at Straumsvík, Norðurál at Grundartangi, Alcoa at Reyðarfjörður). But smelting alumina into P1020 ingot is not the same as making can sheet. The island lacks the downstream rolling and can-making industry, so UBC must leave. Iceland’s lesson is that primary metal capacity does not automatically create a closed beverage-can loop; redemption rate is a collection metric, not a can-to-can remelt metric.

5. Lessons for Puerto Rico

  • Scale is not a barrier if the network is dense enough. A jurisdiction of ~3.2 million people can support a national DRS and a single secondary plant far more easily than Iceland’s 380,000 support their system.
  • A single national operator simplifies governance. Endurvinnslan’s monopoly model reduces coordination costs. PR could appoint one territory-wide scheme coordinator (public or nonprofit) instead of 78 separate municipal programs.
  • Do not confuse primary smelting with recycling. A secondary UBC-to-ingot plant is a different, far less energy-intensive asset than a primary smelter. PR should build the secondary plant first; primary smelting is not required.
  • Keep the deposit simple and uniform. Iceland uses one deposit value for all materials, reducing consumer confusion. PR should start with one clear deposit for aluminum cans and add glass/PET later.

Tasas de recuperación reportadas

Mientras más alto, mejor. La cifra de Islandia (~88 %) es redención/recolección, no circuito cerrado lata-a-lata. PR se incluye como contexto —actual y objetivo.

Cómo se ve la planta

Qué hace la fábrica en realidad

Piensa en ella como una cocina gigante que limpia, funde y moldea latas usadas en bloques sólidos de aluminio.

↻

Camiones traen latas fardadas a una bahía de recepción. Clasificadores ópticos separan el aluminio de otros materiales. Un tambor a gas quema pintura y recubrimientos. Luego un gran horno rotatorio funde el aluminio limpio, y una línea de colada vierte el metal en barras en T llamadas T-bar sow, más lingotes estándar P1020A que se pueden vender en cualquier parte del mundo. El edificio es FORTIFIED para huracanes, y generadores diésel más baterías mantienen la operación durante cortes de red. Los permisos cubren calidad del aire y descarga de agua.

Una sola línea de 15 000 t/año, a gas (la red de PR es ~95 % fósil; el gas domina en emisiones y costo), dentro de un edificio de acero de IBHS, con generación propia dimensionada para soportar 4–8 h de corte de red.

Receiving & sorting

Bahía de recepción de 4 calles, 2 líneas de clasificación óptica MSS para UBC. Capacidad: 3 t/h de alimentación. Proveedor: Van Dyk / MSS.

Despintado

Tambor rotatorio de flujo contracorriente, a gas, con postcombustor CEBA CLEAN RTO para destrucción de COV. Capacidad: 3 t/h. Proveedor: CEBA / Presezzi.

Fundición

Un horno rotativo basculante a gas , carga de 14 m³, tasa de fundición 1,5–8 t/h. para limitar retrasos de entrega. Flujo de bajo contenido de sal (factor 0,2–0,5).

Colada

Línea de colada VDC T-bar Wagstaff. Producto: —compatible con offtake global.

Building & resilience

Edificio de acero FORTIFIED Commercial de 3 500 m². Generador diésel de 2 MW + BESS de 1–2 MWh para respaldo. Opcional 1,5 MW solar en techo y estacionamiento.

Permisos

Permiso menor de aire NSR de JCA, certificación equivalente a NPDES de DRNA, PSD de EPA Región 2 no se activa (capacidad bajo el umbral de 50 kt/año).

Principales riesgos de ingeniería

  1. Retraso en permisos.
  2. Fragilidad de la red.
  3. Exposición a huracanes.
  4. Aluminio fundido + agua.
  5. Retraso en entrega de proveedores.

Los números, completos

Qué significan los números

Cerca de $54 millones para construir, $12 millones al año para operar —y cerca de $32 millones al año en ingresos esperados.

↻

El plan 'mediano' es el recomendado: una planta de 15 000 toneladas/año que costaría cerca de $54 millones construir y $12,3 millones al año operar. En operación estacionaria se proyectan unos $32 millones anuales de ingresos, recuperando la inversión en unos tres años y obteniendo un retorno a largo plazo del 14–18 %. Los costos son algo mayores que en plantas del continente porque la electricidad de Puerto Rico es cara, la construcción debe resistir huracanes y el seguro es más alto. La propuesta compara tamaños pequeño, mediano y grande para que quienes decidan vean las compensaciones.

Tres escenarios de tamaño, línea por línea. El mediano (15 000 t/año) es el caso líder; el pequeño y el grande se mantienen como referencia. Todos los valores son USD 2026 y llevan un rango de planificación de ±25 %. Cada línea documenta su método de estimación en .

Verificación vs. referencias de la industria

Los rangos industriales para plantas verdes de UBC a lingote son de intensidad de CAPEX y de OPEX. El mediano aquí es en CAPEX/t y en OPEX/t —impulsado por el premio de electricidad industrial de PR (2,5× promedio EE. UU.), construcción FORTIFIED (+20–25 % sobre línea base), seguro (2,5 % del CAPEX vs. 0,5 % en continente) y penalidades de envío de la Ley Jones. Un complemento de 1,5 MW solar + 2 MWh BESS cerraría la mayor parte de la brecha de OPEX, dejando el mediano en ~$720/t (dentro de la banda industrial).

La planta sigue siendo financieramente viable porque el lado de ingresos está anclado a un descuento de UBC del 15–20 % sobre LME (~$2 000/t realizados) y un depósito de 5¢ que resuelve la economía de la materia prima de una manera que las plantas del continente no tienen.

Cómo se paga

De dónde viene el dinero

Una mezcla de bono respaldado por depósitos, créditos fiscales federales de energía limpia y capital de inversionistas.

↻

La porción más grande es un bono de ingresos respaldado por los depósitos de 5¢ —cerca de $30 millones. Los créditos fiscales federales de la Ley de Reducción de Inflación añaden unos $11 millones en valor presente: un crédito para el sistema solar y baterías, y otro por producir aluminio reciclado en EE. UU. Subvenciones, capital privado y un préstamo de construcción llenan los vacíos restantes. El costo financiero combinado se estima en ~4,5 %, lo suficientemente bajo para hacer el proyecto atractivo.

Siete fuentes, costo medio de capital ~4,5 % antes de impuestos. El bono de ingresos asegurado por el DRS es la línea más grande y la dependencia de mayor riesgo.

Objetivo: $52,0 M = 100 % del CAPEX mediano (excluyendo 10 % de contingencia, que se mantiene en reserva).

    Qué podría salir mal (y qué hacemos)

    ¿Qué podría salir mal?

    Los mayores riesgos son política, precios del metal, huracanes y retrasos en trámites.

    ↻

    El riesgo número uno es que la ley de depósito de 5¢ nunca se apruebe; el plan B es firmar contratos de materia prima directamente con productores de latas. Los precios del aluminio podrían caer, así que el plan cubre una gran parte de las ventas futuras. Los huracanes son una amenaza real, así que el edificio es reforzado y asegurado. Los permisos podrían tardar más de lo esperado, así que el equipo iniciaría reuniones pre-aplicación temprano. Los retrasos en entrega de equipos se manejan ordenando artículos de largo lead time temprano y teniendo dos proveedores de hornos.

    Clasificados aproximadamente por impacto en el proyecto.

    1. La legislación DRS se estanca.funding-model.md §2.5
    2. Caída del precio del aluminio LME de más del 30 %.
    3. Daños a la planta por huracán.
    4. Retraso de permisos > 12 meses.
    5. Retraso en entrega de proveedores.
    6. Estrechamiento de elegibilidad de créditos IRA.
    7. Escasez de fuerza laboral (electricistas industriales, instrumentación).

    Qué se necesita para construirlo

    Qué debe pasar a continuación

    Tres síes: aprobar la ley de depósito, elegir el sitio y alinear el dinero.

    ↻

    Primero, la Asamblea Legislativa de Puerto Rico debe aprobar el proyecto de depósito-reembolso de 5¢ para que las latas realmente regresen. Segundo, el Gobernador y PRIDCO deben designar el sitio industrial de Ponce e iniciar conversaciones de permisos con EPA, DRNA y JCA. Tercero, inversionistas y prestamistas deben cerrar la pila de financiamiento de cerca de $52 millones. Si esas tres cosas se alinean, la construcción podría comenzar en 2028 y la planta alcanzar producción plena alrededor de 2031.

    El proyecto es financiable, construible y operable. El cuello de botella es voluntad política, no ingeniería ni capital. Tres solicitudes, en orden de urgencia.

    El caso económico en un párrafo

    Cada año PR exporta ~17 000 t de UBC e importa de vuelta las latas resultantes. El sistema actual fuga capital, empleos, energía y material de la isla. Cerrar el ciclo retiene unos en la isla, crea , evita vs. aluminio primario virgen y reduce el déficit comercial de PR en aproximadamente la misma cifra que los ingresos de la planta. El DRS de 5¢ es la entrada; la planta es la captura de valor en la isla.
