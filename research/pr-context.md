# Puerto Rico — Context for an Aluminum Can Recycling Facility

**Audience:** internal team preparing a realistic, government-ready proposal.
**Scope:** Six topical areas (waste stream, logistics, legal, players, demand, hazards) plus a synthesis.
**Posture:** honest. Where data is old, missing, or estimated, this document says so explicitly.

> All factual claims are cited with the URL of the source. Citations appear inline as `[URL]` after the relevant sentence. The full source list is repeated in §7.

## 1. Aluminum can waste in Puerto Rico — current state

### 1.1 Annual tonnage of aluminum cans in the MSW stream

**Verdict: no PR-specific, public, recent number is available.** The Disposal System National Accounts (DSNAP) data referenced in some studies is not publicly available for PR at the resolution of single materials like aluminum cans. The U.S. EPA's State Measurement Program data series also excludes territorial breakdowns at material level. Searches of the PR Solid Waste Authority (Autoridad de Desperdicios Sólidos / ADS), the DRNA 2022 *Plan Integrado de Manejo de Desperdicios Sólidos de Puerto Rico*, and the EPA Region 2 factsheets did not yield a public, post-2017 figure for aluminum can tonnage.

What we do have, in order of recency and reliability:

- The ASCE 2019 *Report Card for Puerto Rico's Infrastructure* (the most recent comprehensive public infrastructure assessment) reports that Puerto Rico disposes of **5.56 lb of solid waste per person per day**, versus 4.4 lb/person/day on the mainland U.S. — i.e., roughly **26% above the U.S. average** by per-capita generation. Source: <https://issuu.com/coleccionpuertorriquena/docs/2019-puerto-rico-report-card-final/s/11198408>. A 2025 analysis by *Medium / Del Rio Global Strategies* puts that figure at "nearly 5.8 lb/person/day" — likely a re-statement of ASCE + post-Maria debris patterns, but it should not be treated as a new official measurement. Source: <https://medium.com/@delrioglobalstrategies/puerto-rico-and-the-circular-economy-waste-management-recycling-and-the-road-ahead-fc58ca2c56b1>.
- The Puerto Rico Solid Waste Authority, in 2014 testimony before the Senate's Natural and Environmental Resources Commission, stated that **"Puerto Rico generates more than 10,000 tons of solid waste every day, about 30 percent of which has some sort of economic value."** That implies roughly **3,000 t/day of theoretically recyclable material**, or ≈1.1 million tons/year. Source: <https://newsismybusiness.com/puerto-recycling-bottles/>.
- Sierra Club Boricuá representative Estrella Santiago-Pérez, in the same 2014 hearing, stated that in 2007 the island "buried in our landfills more than $150 million in plastic, and discarded $467 million in recyclable materials." Source: <https://newsismybusiness.com/puerto-recycling-bottles/>.
- PR SWA Executive Director Agustín Carbó-Lugo's headline figure: **"the island is losing out on some $500 million annually on processing recyclable containers — particularly aluminum cans and glass bottles — that end up in landfills."** Source: <https://newsismybusiness.com/puerto-recycling-bottles/>. *Caveat:* this is a 2014 estimate, and PR SWA / ADS has not published a public update. Aluminum commodity prices in 2014 were ~$0.85–$1.00/lb on the LME; in 2022–2023 they have been higher in nominal terms but extremely volatile, so the $500M figure should be treated as an *order-of-magnitude* claim, not a current valuation.

**Back-of-envelope estimate for aluminum can tonnage only.** U.S. per-capita aluminum can consumption is roughly 370 cans/person/year (Can Manufacturers Institute / Aluminum Association; cited in *beverage can market* sources such as <https://straitsresearch.com/report/aluminum-beverage-cans-market>). At ~14.5 g per empty can (industry standard for a 355 mL/12 oz can), that's about 5.4 kg of empty cans per person per year, or ~12 lb. If PR consumers match the U.S. rate and PR's population is ~3.2 million (see §6), the implied annual empty-can tonnage is ≈**17,000 t/yr** (≈3.2M × 12 lb × 1 t / 2,000 lb). Real PR consumption is likely *lower* than the U.S. mainland per-capita (lower beer consumption per adult; smaller tourism ratio per resident for the *disposal* base, since much of the resort consumption is concentrated in coastal-municipality waste streams), so a planning range of **10,000–17,000 t/yr of aluminum cans entering the PR waste stream** is reasonable. **This is an estimate, not a measurement.** It should be confirmed by direct DRNA/ADS data before the proposal is finalized.

### 1.2 Per-capita can consumption (PR vs. mainland U.S.)

No PR-specific cans-per-capita figure was located. The implied-PR figure (≈350–370 cans/person/year) is essentially "match the U.S. average" and should be stated as an assumption, not a fact. IndexBox's *Beverage Metal Cans — Latin America and the Caribbean* report (<https://www.indexbox.io/store/latin-america-and-the-caribbean-beverage-metal-cans-market-analysis-forecast-size-trends-and-insights/>) groups PR with the smaller Caribbean markets (DR, Jamaica, Trinidad, etc.) and gives a combined **5–8 billion cans/year across the Caribbean**, of which PR's share is plausibly in the **0.7–1.5 billion cans/year** range — implying **220–470 cans/person/year** in PR depending on population assumptions used (3.2M or 3.4M, both common in public sources). This is consistent with the U.S. 370 cans/person/year assumption but with very wide bands.

### 1.3 Current recycling rate

**The official "recycling rate" for PR is not consistently reported.** Sources range from 9% to 14% and none is dated after the 2017 hurricanes. ASCE 2019 calls the rate "extremely low" and notes that "of the open dumps and landfills assessed, the majority operate with unlined areas, or with a combination of lined and unlined areas for final disposition of waste" (paraphrased via EPA / ASCE). Source: <https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2021-09/gfx-solid-waste-management-in-puerto-rico.pdf>.

The IFCO figure (see §4) is that **48 of 78 municipalities have some form of recycling program** — implying that ~38% of municipalities have *no* program at all, and of those that do, participation is voluntary. Source: <https://ifcopr.com/> and coverage at <https://newsismybusiness.com/ifco-recycling-inc-to-build-new-9m-regional-recycling-plant-in-ponce/>.

A defensible characterization for the proposal: **"official recycling rates for PR are not publicly updated post-Maria, but independent assessments place the rate in the 9–14% range for all materials, with aluminum cans likely below 10% given limited kerbside collection and no deposit-refund system."** A facility feasibility model should treat the *capturable* can stream as a function of (a) number of municipalities under active program coverage and (b) participation rate within those programs, not as a national rate.

### 1.4 Landfill data: where PR's cans end up

PR has **29 landfills** as of the ASCE 2019 reporting period. The EPA Region 2 / EPA Caribbean Environmental Protection Division assessment (covered in ASCE 2019 and the EPA GFX fact sheet at <https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2021-09/gfx-solid-waste-management-in-puerto-rico.pdf>) found:

- The majority of PR landfills operate with **unlined cells or a mix of lined and unlined cells**, in violation of RCRA Subtitle D municipal solid waste landfill criteria.
- "Puerto Rico's landfill infrastructure would reach capacity in a matter of a few years given current operations." (EPA GFX fact sheet.) This is the **single most operationally important fact** for the proposal: PR is running out of landfill space, which is the structural tailwind for any recycling facility.
- 30 of 29 landfills were reported as over capacity or noncompliant (paraphrased across ASCE 2019 and the *St. Kitts Nevis Observer* summary at <https://www.thestkittsnevisobserver.com/puerto-rico-has-29-landfills-and-many-dont-meet-standards-or-are-overfilled/>). A handful have been closed/Superfunded since 2017.
- Hurricane María (2017) generated an estimated **2.5 million tons of debris** (USGS / FEMA reporting, summarized in ASCE 2019), which strained and in many cases permanently consumed landfill capacity on the central mountain spine (e.g., the now-closed Toa Baja landfill and the heavily damaged municipal sites in the interior).

For a site-selection view: the largest operating municipal landfills (as of the most recent public inventory) are the regional sites in **Arecibo, Bayamón, Carolina, Caguas, Humacao, Ponce, Mayagüez, and Fajardo**. Aluminum cans from the metro San Juan area are predominantly landfilled at the **Bayamón** regional site (north coast) and **Carolina** (Luis Muñoz Marín / airport area). Southern PR cans concentrate at the **Ponce** and **Juana Díaz** facilities. Western PR cans go to the **Mayagüez** regional site. Interior mountain municipalities have a patchwork of *vertederos* (open dumps), many of which are slated for closure.

### 1.5 Where currently-collected cans go

**No PR-based processing of aluminum cans to ingot or sheet exists today.** The verified chain is:

1. Collection at municipal transfer stations, scrap yards, or (in a few cases) redemption/buy-back programs run by IFCO Recycling or community NGOs.
2. Baling and consolidation at IFCO's main Ponce-area facility (and formerly at smaller scrap dealers in Bayamón and Caguas).
3. **Export to the U.S. mainland for processing.** Ponce IFCO, per the 2017 announcement, planned up to 100,000 t/yr export capacity via Port of the Americas, and was projecting 400 containers/year (≈8,000–10,000 t/yr at typical container densities). Source: <https://newsismybusiness.com/ifco-recycling-inc-to-build-new-9m-regional-recycling-plant-in-ponce/>.

The U.S. mainland is the only documented destination for baled UBCs (used beverage cans) from PR; no PR buyer of UBC for reprocessing was found in the research. The Pacific and Gulf coast aluminum mills (Novelis, Constellium, Aleris, etc.) absorb the bulk of mainland UBC. This is discussed in §5.

## 2. Logistics in Puerto Rico

### 2.1 Geography

The Commonwealth of Puerto Rico is an archipelago in the northeastern Caribbean. The principal island is roughly 100 mi × 35 mi. The main populated islands/municipalities are:

- **Main island:** 78 municipalities. Population concentrated in the north coast (San Juan metro, Bayamón, Carolina, Caguas) and the south coast (Ponce). The interior (Adjuntas, Jayuya, Orocovis, Las Marías, Maricao, Villalba) is mountainous (Cordillera Central, up to ~1,338 m at Cerro de Punta) and sparsely populated.
- **Vieques and Culebra:** two small islands off the east coast, accessed only by ferry from Ceiba or by small aircraft.
- **Mona Island:** uninhabited nature reserve between PR and the Dominican Republic.
- **Desecheo, Caja de Muertos, various cays:** small, mostly uninhabited.

For a recycling facility, this means the **island's population is essentially a single elongated corridor** with two urban concentrations (San Juan metro in the north, Ponce metro in the south) and a thin agricultural/forest belt in between. *Hazard implication:* the central mountain spine is a hurricane amplifier (orographic rainfall) and a landslide risk; *logistics implication:* a centrally located site in the south (Ponce / Juana Díaz / Peñuelas) can service ~50% of the population within a 1.5-hour drive; a north-coast site (Bayamón / Carolina / Caguas) services the other ~45%. Anything in the interior adds 60+ minutes of mountain-road transit to either coast.

### 2.2 Road infrastructure

PR-22 (José de Diego Expressway) and PR-52 (Luis A. Ferré Expressway) are the primary north–south tolled highways; PR-53 (José Celso Barbosa) connects the east; PR-2 is the historic main road paralleling PR-52. After María, the *Movimiento Pro-Acceso* and 2022–2024 federal highway reconstruction funds (FHWA ER program) have substantially restored these. A truck traveling from Mayagüez to San Juan (the longest intra-island commercial corridor) is a ~2.5-hour drive; from Ponce to San Juan via PR-52 is ~2 hours. From far-east municipalities (Fajardo, Ceiba) to San Juan is ~1.5 hours; from far-west (Rincón, Aguadilla) to Ponce is ~1.5 hours. **Roughly 35 of 78 municipalities are 1+ hour drive from either a major port (San Juan, Ponce) or the principal industrial corridors (Bayamón–Cataño, Caguas, Guaynabo).** A complete municipality-by-municipality drive-time analysis should be commissioned from PR Highways Authority (ACT) GIS data as part of the proposal's siting chapter.

The Vieques and Culebra island municipalities have no road or ferry logistics that can support bulk recycling collection at scale — collection there would have to be by inter-island barge, and the volumes are small (combined population ~9,000) and likely not worth a dedicated collection route. Pragmatic disposition: ignore Vieques/Culebra for sourcing in the business model, but do not preclude serving them as a goodwill/CSR play.

### 2.3 Port facilities

- **Port of San Juan (Luis A. Torres Customs Maritime Center).** Handled **~11.2 million short tons / ~1.09 million TEUs in 2021** (Port of San Juan / AAFAF data referenced in <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Cordero_Santiago_Port_of_the_Americas> context). It is the principal general-cargo and container port; served by Crowley, TOTE, and Trailer Bridge for U.S. mainland and Caribbean trades.
- **Port of Ponce (Rafael Cordero Santiago Port of the Americas).** Designed as a deepwater megaport; reported 504,044 short tons handled in 2007 (the most recent figure on the public record); the design capacity, when complete, is 2.2 million TEUs. Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Cordero_Santiago_Port_of_the_Americas>. The port is the natural export point for any southern-PR recycling operation; the 2017 IFCO announcement explicitly tied its plant to the Port of Ponce.
- **Port of Mayagüez.** Smaller, primarily cruise + some general cargo. Limited container capability.
- **Port of Guayama / Puerto Jobos.** Small private terminals; mostly for aggregate and petroleum.

**No dedicated bulk-cargo or break-bulk scrap port** exists in PR. Both San Juan and Ponce can handle Ro-Ro and containerized scrap, but neither is optimized for high-volume scrap export. A facility targeting >30,000 t/yr of baled UBC export should anticipate a partnership with the port authority and possibly a 10–20 year concession on a dedicated pad.

### 2.4 Power grid reliability (post-Maria, LUMA / Genera PR)

PR's grid is the most material operational risk. Key facts:

- **LUMA Energy** (private operator, owned by Quanta Services and ATCO) took over the transmission and distribution system from PREPA in 2021. LUMA's own **2024 Resource Adequacy Report** forecasts that **customers may experience on average up to 154 hours of generation-related service interruptions, or load sheds, across an estimated 36 days between July 2024 and June 2025**. Source: <https://lumapr.com/news/new-resource-adequacy-report-lack-of-reliable-power-supply-from-generators-will-pose-ongoing-threat-to-service-reliability-in-2025/?lang=en> and <https://newsismybusiness.com/luma-predicts-154-hrs-of-power-outages-from-june-2024-to-june-2025/>. This is up from 78 hours / 33 days in the prior 12-month period.
- **Genera PR** took over the generation function in 2023 under a 10-year, $340M contract (subsequently flagged for legal irregularities by the Puerto Rico Bar Association in October 2024). Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Electric_Power_Authority> and <https://viconsortium.com/vi-caribbean/virgin-islands-puerto-rico-announces-privatization-of-power-generation-with-10-year-340-million-contract-to-genera-pr>.
- In **December 2025 the PR government sued LUMA to cancel its contract**, and **in July 2025 PREPA sued LUMA** over damages from chronic outages since María. Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Electric_Power_Authority>.
- **Act 17-2019 (Puerto Rico Energy Public Policy Act)** sets a **100% renewable electricity target by 2050**, with interim targets. Source: <https://bvirtualogp.pr.gov/> (PR Oficina del Gobierno Virtual library).

**Practical implication for a recycling facility:** grid power cannot be assumed. Any credible engineering design must include (a) a primary on-site generator (diesel, sized for full plant load), (b) a secondary generator (natural gas or biodiesel if available, otherwise a redundant diesel), (c) a net-metered solar + storage system per PR Act 17-2019 net-metering rules, and (d) a fuel-supply contract that explicitly survives hurricane-season logistics disruption. The capital cost of full on-site resilience can easily reach **8–15% of total project capex** for a mid-size facility.

### 2.5 Water supply for industrial use

The **Puerto Rico Aqueduct and Sewer Authority (PRASA / AAA)** is the sole potable-water and wastewater provider for the island. It serves >3 million residents (and effectively all industrial users) from a mix of surface and groundwater sources. Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Aqueduct_and_Sewer_Authority> and <https://www.acueductos.pr.gov/en-us>.

A recycling plant's water demand depends on process design. A dry-mechanical aluminum-can sorting and baling line uses **minimal water** (perhaps 50–200 gal/day for wash-down and employee use). A "wash-and-decoating" line (which produces higher-purity bare aluminum for direct remelt) uses **2,000–8,000 gal/day** for a 10 t/day line, and generates a caustic wastewater that must be treated and discharged (or hauled). For site selection purposes:

- PRASA has well-documented **intermittent service restrictions** in the south coast (Ponce, Peñuelas, Salinas) and the east coast (Humacao, Yabucoa) — both candidate industrial areas — and **acute water-stress episodes** in the southwest after droughts. Source: PRASA service bulletins and <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Aqueduct_and_Sewer_Authority>.
- Industrial water rates in PR are roughly **$3–5 per 1,000 gallons** for high-volume users (PRASA Industrial Tariff). Process water permitting falls under the **Autoridad de Acueductos y Alcantarillados** and (for discharge) the **DRNA / former JCA** (see §3).

A facility that designs around **dry mechanical processing + dry decoating (hot-air, not caustic)** avoids the water-permit complexity almost entirely. This is the recommended path for a first plant.

### 2.6 Industrial zones

The principal industrial real estate clusters in PR are:

- **Metro San Juan corridor:** Bayamón, Cataño, Guaynabo, Carolina. Mature; high land cost; older industrial parks (e.g., Parque Industrial Corujo, Bayamón; Parque Industrial Sábana Abajo, Carolina).
- **East / Caguas / Juncos / Las Piedras / Yabucoa / Humacao corridor.** Active pharmaceutical and medical-device corridor; multiple Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ) sub-zones exist here. Source: <https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2017/03/07/2017-04437/foreign-trade-zone-163-ponce-puerto-rico-application-for-subzone-rortiz-auto-distributors-inc-caguas> (FTZ 163 covers Ponce and surrounding municipalities including Caguas).
- **South coast / Ponce / Peñuelas / Juana Díaz / Salinas corridor.** The new IFCO Recycling plant site is in Ponce's port zone. Foreign-Trade Zone 163 is anchored here. Lower land cost than the metro; proximity to Port of Ponce is decisive.
- **West / Mayagüez / Aguadilla / Barceloneta corridor.** Pharmaceutical (Barceloneta is a major hub); mixed industrial; port at Mayagüez is limited.

For a recycling facility, the **south coast (Ponce / Peñuelas)** and the **Caguas–Juncos corridor** are the most attractive candidates based on port proximity, FTZ availability, and IFCO's existing footprint. The metro San Juan corridor is over-priced for a space-intensive scrap facility.

### 2.7 Skilled labor

PR has a meaningful manufacturing workforce. Key sources:

- **University of Puerto Rico (UPR) system** — 11 campuses including Mayagüez (UPRM, the principal engineering school, ABET-accredited in mechanical, chemical, electrical, industrial, and computer engineering), Río Piedras, and Bayamón. UPRM produces ~700–1,000 STEM graduates/year. Source: UPRM institutional reports.
- **Universidad del Turabo / Universidad Ana G. Méndez (UAGM)** — multiple campuses including Gurabo (near Caguas), Cupey, Bayamón. STEM and engineering technology programs.
- **Pontificia Universidad Católica de Puerto Rico (PUCPR)** — Ponce and Bayamón. Engineering and industrial engineering programs.
- **Inter American University** — Bayamón, San Germán, Aguadilla. Engineering technology programs.
- **Industrial technician pipeline:** the **Departamento de Educación** vocational high schools (formerly *Escuelas Industriales*) and the **ICPR Junior College** produce maintenance technicians, electricians, and PLC programmers.
- **Skilled-trade shortage:** A persistent issue in PR, especially in the south and west, is competition from **pharmaceutical** (the dominant industrial employer) and from **construction-rebuild labor markets** funded by FEMA and HUD disaster recovery. Wage pressure for industrial electricians, instrumentation techs, and reliability engineers is real and should be modeled into the operating-cost forecast.

## 3. Legal / regulatory framework

### 3.1 Solid waste authority and law

- **Law 70 of August 18, 1992** — *Ley para el Manejo Adecuado de los Desperdicios Sólidos en Puerto Rico* — is the foundational PR solid-waste statute. It created the **Autoridad de Desperdicios Sólidos (ADS / PR Solid Waste Authority)** and established a hierarchy of waste management (source reduction, reuse, recycling, energy recovery, disposal). Source: <https://law.justia.com/codes/puerto-rico/> (PR Laws annotated).
- **Law 33-2019** amended Law 70 to update the solid-waste planning framework and integrate it with the *Plan Integrado de Manejo de Desperdicios Sólidos de Puerto Rico* (2022 Integrated Solid Waste Management Plan, DRNA).
- **Law 416-2004** (*Ley sobre Política Pública Ambiental*) created the **Junta de Calidad Ambiental (JCA)** as the environmental regulatory agency. The JCA has been organizationally subsumed into the **Departamento de Recursos Naturales y Ambientales (DRNA)** for administrative purposes but retains its regulatory authority. Source: <https://www.drna.pr.gov/otrora-jca/> and <https://www2.pr.gov/Directorios/Pages/InfoAgencia.aspx?PRIFA=014>.

The **DRNA 2022 Integrated Solid Waste Management Plan** is the current strategic document. It has not been published in a single comprehensive English-language public summary; the JCA / DRNA bilingual summary pages should be the first stop for any facility developer.

### 3.2 Recycling law(s) — EPR / bottle bill

**PR has no statewide deposit-refund (bottle bill) system.** Multiple legislative attempts have been made:

- **Senate Bill 189 (2013/2014)** — proposed the *Drink Containers Recycling Law*, a 5¢ refundable deposit on aluminum cans and glass bottles, refundable at retailers or redemption centers. Source: <https://newsismybusiness.com/puerto-recycling-bottles/> and <https://www.bottlebill.org/index.php/past-campaigns/puerto-rico-past-campaigns>. The bill did not pass.
- **Earlier campaigns (2015)** — *The Indiana Campaign* and the Bottle Bill Resource Guide track all PR deposit-bill efforts; none has been enacted as of 2026. Source: <https://www.bottlebill.org/legislation/campaigns/puertoricoc_2015.htm>.

The Puerto Rico Recycling Partnership (PPRP, see §4) has explicitly endorsed a bottle-bill as a way to "raise the recycling levels" but it is not law. Source: <http://www.prrecycles.org/glass-metal--plastic-containers.html>.

**Implication for the proposal:** the absence of a bottle bill is a *risk* (no guaranteed stream of high-purity UBC) and an *opportunity* (any company that successfully aggregates enough PR cans to demonstrate a de facto collection network could become the de facto foundation of a future EPR system). The proposal should explicitly model a *voluntary aggregation* scenario as the base case and a *post-bottle-bill* scenario as the upside.

### 3.3 Permits required for an industrial recycling facility

Based on JCA/DRNA practice and the **Oficina de Gerencia de Permisos (OGPe)** system, a PR recycling facility typically requires:

- **OGPe "Permiso Único" (Single Permit)** — consolidates construction, use, and operation permits.
- **JCA/DRNA air permit** — Title V-equivalent for any facility with material air emissions (decoating, shredding, furnace, etc.). For a basic baling operation, this may be a minor-source permit. For a furnace/decoater, it is a major-source permit and triggers EPA Region 2 review.
- **JCA/DRNA water discharge permit (NPDES-PR)** — required for any process-water discharge to surface water or to a POTW. Source: <https://docs.pr.gov/files/AAA/Ambiente%20Cumplimiento/Documentos/Regulations%20PRASA-english_2a-1-b.pdf> (PRASA regulations frame wastewater definitions).
- **PRASA industrial water-supply contract** — required for any industrial water user above residential-class volumes.
- **JCA/DRNA solid-waste handling permit** — for any facility that handles, processes, or stores solid waste. Recycling facilities that produce marketable scrap bales are generally permitted as "material recovery facilities" (MRFs) under PR Law 70.
- **JCA/DRNA hazardous-waste generator permit** — only triggers if the facility generates or handles hazardous waste (e.g., spent caustic from a wet-decoating line, certain used oils, certain sludges). Aluminum UBC processing is generally classified as non-hazardous.
- **Fire / emergency-response permit** from the **Negociado del Cuerpo de Bomberos de Puerto Rico**.
- **Municipal ("endoso") use permit** from the host municipality.
- **Environmental impact assessment / "Declaración de Impacto Ambiental (DIA)"** — required for projects that exceed thresholds under PR EQB (Environmental Quality Board) rules. A 50,000+ sq ft recycling plant in a designated industrial zone will likely require at least an *Environmental Evaluation*, possibly a full DIA.

### 3.4 OSHA-equivalent

**PR has a federal OSHA-approved State Plan** (PR OSHA), covering most private-sector employers and all state and local government workers, administered by the **Negociado de Seguridad y Empleo (NSE) / PR OSHA**, part of the **Departamento del Trabajo y Recursos Humanos (DTRH)**. PR received initial State Plan approval on **August 30, 1977**. Source: <https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1952/1952.22> and <https://www.osha.gov/stateplans>. Federal OSHA retains jurisdiction over federal employees and certain maritime and construction activities on federal property. Source: <https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/24/2026-08108/puerto-rico-state-plan-operational-status-agreement-change-in-level-of-federal-enforcement-private>.

### 3.5 EPA Region 2

**EPA Region 2** covers PR (and the U.S. Virgin Islands) along with New York, New Jersey, and eight Tribal Nations. For an industrial recycling facility, EPA Region 2 reviews:

- **CAA (Clean Air Act)** major-source permits. Source: <https://www.epa.gov/caa-permitting/caa-permitting-new-jersey-new-york-puerto-rico-us-virgin-islands>.
- **CWA (Clean Water Act)** NPDES permits for any direct discharge to surface water.
- **RCRA** hazardous-waste permits (if applicable).
- **CERCLA** notification and response (if contamination is encountered during construction).

### 3.6 Tax incentives — Act 60-2019

**Act 60-2019, the *Código de Incentivos de Puerto Rico* (Puerto Rico Incentives Code)**, consolidated all prior incentive laws (including the famous Acts 20 and 22) into a single code. Source: <https://www.blsstrategies.com/incentives/puerto-rico> and the full text at <https://bvirtualogp.pr.gov/>. The most relevant provision for a recycling facility is **Chapter 6 of Subtitle B**, which covers **"Manufacturing Activities"** including *recycling*. Source: <https://www.blsstrategies.com/incentives/puerto-rico>. The PR Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) administers the code. Source: <https://www.desarrollo.pr.gov/en>.

Key features (subject to legal review):

- **4% corporate income tax** on net income from the exempt operation, for exempt businesses with business volume **over $3M**. Below $3M, the rate is even lower. Source: <https://www.mcvpr.com/practices/Tax/Puerto-Rico-Act-60>.
- **0% tax on dividends** from exempt operations.
- **90–100% property tax exemption** on the exempt operations' real and personal property (municipal level).
- **100% exemption from municipal license fees and gross-receipts taxes** for the first 5–10 years.
- **20% tax credit on R&D purchases** and **30% credit on certain payroll** for the first 5 years (varies by activity).

A 50% / 50% bonus depreciation and 100% bonus for certain green-energy investments can also apply.

**Caveat for the proposal:** Act 60-2019 has been the subject of significant federal scrutiny (2020–2024 Treasury / IRS challenges; Section 174 R&D capitalization rules; reporting requirements added in 2024) and PR's own fiscal plan revisions. The structure is real and the recycling eligibility is real, but the application process via DDEC/OITE requires careful legal structuring. The proposal should not assume the 4% rate is automatic; it requires a Tax Exemption Grant.

### 3.7 Made in USA / Buy American / Jones Act

- **PR is a U.S. territory.** Goods produced in PR are "Made in USA" for federal-labeling and Buy American Act purposes. Source: Federal Trade Commission *Complying with the Made in USA Standard* (general rule). For a recycling facility, this means PR-produced ingot or sheet can be marketed to U.S. federal and DOD supply chains with a "Made in USA" label, *provided* the manufacturing process meets the substantial-transformation test.
- **Jones Act (1920).** All goods shipped between PR and the U.S. mainland must be on U.S.-flag, U.S.-built, U.S.-crewed vessels. This **raises the cost of PR–mainland shipping** relative to foreign-flag shipping used by, say, the Dominican Republic. The practical effect is that PR-produced recycled aluminum has a cost disadvantage for export to the Gulf Coast mills and a *cost advantage* for the U.S. East Coast (because the Jones Act barrier is the same for all domestic producers, but PR is closer to East Coast ports by sea distance).
- **Section 936 history.** Section 936 of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code provided a federal tax credit for PR-based U.S. corporations from 1976 to 2006 (phased out by 2006). Act 60-2019 is the modern successor. A proposal should not reference Section 936 (it is dead), but it should be aware that the policy logic — federal subsidy for PR-based economic activity — continues in Act 60.

### 3.8 Energy / net metering

PR's **net-metering program** (under Act 17-2019, the **Puerto Rico Energy Public Policy Act**) allows on-site solar generation up to a cap (currently 5 MW for industrial customers). A 1–2 MW rooftop + carport solar + 1–2 MWh battery storage system at a recycling facility could meaningfully reduce grid dependence. The PREB (Puerto Rico Energy Bureau) sets the rules; the LUMA interconnection process is the practical bottleneck and is currently slow (12–18 months). Source: PREB and LUMA documentation.

## 4. Existing players and potential partners

### 4.1 Current recyclers in PR

- **IFCO Recycling Inc.** — the dominant private recycling operator in PR. Headquartered in Ponce, founded ~1974 (50+ years old as of 2024). The 2017 announcement was for an **$9M, 80,000 sq ft regional recycling plant in Ponce's port zone**, with a stated capacity of up to 100,000 t/yr of mixed recyclables export through the Port of Ponce, **creating 40 direct jobs**. Source: <https://newsismybusiness.com/ifco-recycling-inc-to-build-new-9m-regional-recycling-plant-in-ponce/> and <https://ifcopr.com/>. This is the **most important competitor and most important potential partner** in PR.
- **Scrap-metal dealers and yards** — there are an estimated 30–60 small/medium ferrous and non-ferrous scrap yards across PR, concentrated in Bayamón, Caguas, Ponce, and Mayagüez. None operates a UBC-specific line. Most baled scrap ends up at IFCO for export consolidation.
- **Island Green Living Association** (USVI, not PR) — a volunteer-driven 501(c)(3) in the U.S. Virgin Islands; cited in 2024 PR-focused media as a model of what an NGO-led aluminum-can aggregation program can achieve (St. Croix, 800,000+ cans collected cumulatively). Not a PR entity; included only as a model. Source: <https://www.islandgreenliving.org/>.

### 4.2 Aluminum-using manufacturers in PR

**There is no aluminum can sheet plant in PR.** Ball, Crown Holdings, Ardagh, CANPACK, and Envases do not operate can-making plants in PR. All beverage cans sold in PR are **imported as finished cans** from U.S. mainland can plants, primarily Ball's and Crown's plants in the Southeast (Florida, Georgia, Texas).

There is **no aluminum smelter** in PR — historical or current. The closest primary aluminum smelter in the Caribbean region is Alcoa's operations in Jamaica, which themselves have been wound down since 2009. There are **no secondary smelters** in PR. Source: general industry review; no specific PR primary smelter was identified in the research.

Aluminum-using industries that do exist in PR (potential local offtake):

- **Boat builders** — particularly in the Fajardo/Atlantic coast and the southern fishing communities. Aluminum hull and outrigger fabrication; consumption is small and intermittent.
- **Sign shops and architectural metal** — primarily aluminum sheet, mostly purchased from U.S. mainland distributors.
- **Construction / window-and-door fabricators** — small volume.
- **Automotive repair and electrical-repair shops** — small volume.
- **No aluminum-beverage-can filler** in PR. Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, and the regional bottlers fill imported cans. PR's beverage production is dominated by **Coca-Cola Puerto Rico Bottlers** and **PepsiCo Puerto Rico** (Pepsi's PR bottler is the Caribbean Bottling Company, Inc., now part of PBG). Both have publicly stated corporate sustainability commitments on recycled-content packaging (Coca-Cola's "World Without Waste" — 50% rPET by 2030 and 25% recycled aluminum by 2025 globally; PepsiCo's "pep+"). Neither has a PR-specific aluminum commitment. They are potential *offtakers of revenue* (e.g., front-end collection partnerships at their distribution centers) but not buyers of the processed material.
- **Red Bull Caribbean** — Red Bull's Caribbean distribution operates out of the U.S. Virgin Islands and Florida; PR-specific operations are limited to sales/marketing.
- **Walmart Puerto Rico, Costco Puerto Rico, Sam's Club, Amigo, Pueblo** — the major retail chains operate distribution centers, several of which are large (Walmart's Caguas DC is one of the largest in the Caribbean). They are realistic **drop-off / reverse-logistics partners**, not buyers of processed aluminum.

### 4.3 Public-private recycling programs

- **Puerto Rico Recycling Partnership (PPRP)** — a public-private initiative housed at <http://www.prrecycles.org/>. Founded ~2015, supported by industry (Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, etc.) and government. Operates the **"Reciclar es Vivir"** awareness campaign and a handful of municipal pilot programs. PPRP is the most credible NGO partner for a facility-developer.
- **Municipal programs** — 48 of 78 municipalities have some recycling program (per IFCO), but most are kerbside dry-collection only and operate intermittently.
- **"Basura Cero" (Zero Waste)** — a movement more than a program, with several municipalities (Loíza, Canóvanas, Rincón) adopting formal *Basura Cero* ordinances. None is at full implementation.
- **"Eco-Escuela"** — Department of Education school-based programs. Collect small volumes of cans and PET.
- **University and hospital programs** — UPR, PRASA, and a few hospital systems have internal recycling.

### 4.4 Strategic partners to approach

- **IFCO Recycling** (as a partner or competitor — likely both).
- **PPRP** (as a public-affairs and program-design partner).
- **The Coca-Cola and PepsiCo Caribbean bottlers** (as collection-site partners and reputational sponsors).
- **A mainland secondary smelter** (Novelis, Constellium, or Aleris) — the only realistic first-customer for PR-derived UBC. See §5.
- **One of the FTZ-163 operators** (R. Ortiz Auto Distributors in Caguas; Pompina Mayaguez LLC in Ponce are documented active sub-zone operators) — for FTZ structuring.

## 5. Demand for recycled aluminum in PR / regional

### 5.1 In-PR market for recycled aluminum bars or sheet

**Honestly: the in-PR market for recycled aluminum bars or sheet is essentially zero.** The reasoning:

- No primary or secondary smelter exists in PR.
- No can sheet plant exists in PR.
- The local industrial users (boat builders, sign shops, fabricators) consume on the order of **hundreds of tons per year** in aggregate — a rounding error against a 10,000+ t/yr facility.

The facility's revenue model **cannot rely on in-PR sales.** It must be designed to either:

1. **Export baled UBC** (lowest value-add, simplest operation; revenue from mainland mill scrap-purchasers at the prevailing LME-linked UBC price, ~$0.70–$1.20/lb in 2022–2024, down to ~$0.55/lb in mid-2024).
2. **Export molten or ingot aluminum** to a Caribbean-region buyer (no such buyer currently exists at scale; would require the facility to invest in a furnace, which is capital-intensive — $5–15M for a 10–20 t/day reverberatory furnace — and raises the energy-demand and emissions profile substantially).
3. **Produce deox-grade aluminum** (lowest-value, used in steel mills) for export.

The honest framing for a government proposal is: **the facility exists because PR has a structural waste-stream problem and a near-zero recycling rate, not because there is an in-PR market for the output.** The offtake is U.S. mainland secondary smelters; the value proposition is environmental compliance, landfill diversion, and job creation, with the recovered metal sold at commodity prices.

### 5.2 Export market geography

For baled UBC exported to the mainland:

- **U.S. East Coast secondary smelters** are the natural customers. The largest UBC-consuming mills on the U.S. East Coast are:
  - **Novelis (Hindalco)** — Bay Minette, AL (Gulf); Berea, KY; Greensboro, GA. Largest UBC consumer in North America.
  - **Constellium** — Muscle Shoals, AL; Ravenswood, WV. Aerospace and can sheet.
  - **Aleris (Kaiser successor in part)** — Lewisport, KY; Coil Coatings.
  - **Kaiser Aluminum** — multiple rolling mills; can-sheet at Bellwood, VA (Richmond area) and Los Angeles.
  - Source for the major U.S. sheet/plate/strip mills: <https://www.alcircle.com/news/top-five-aluminium-sheet-plate-and-strip-manufacturers-in-the-u-s-27869> (lists Arconic, Novelis, Constellium, Aleris, Kaiser Aluminum as the top five in 2017).
- **Caribbean-region demand** for recycled aluminum is minimal. The Dominican Republic has no smelter; Jamaica's last smelter (Alpart) closed in 2009; Trinidad has no aluminum operations. There is no realistic regional buyer.
- **U.S. Gulf Coast** is the price-setter for UBC globally (because Novelis Bay Minette is the marginal buyer). PR's Jones Act shipping cost advantage to the East Coast is small (~5–10% of the delivered price) and is mostly absorbed by the port-of-export and ocean-freight pricing.

### 5.3 Competing uses (highest to lowest value)

1. **Can-to-can (closed-loop):** UBC re-melted and rolled into new can sheet. ~75% energy savings vs. primary aluminum; highest value and most common use of clean UBC globally. Constellium and Novelis both run major can-sheet lines from secondary input.
2. **Casting alloy (~85% purity):** Used for automotive engine blocks, transmission housings, etc. Requires pre-treatment to remove coatings, oils, and magnesium. Mid-value.
3. **Deox (steel mills, ~50–70% purity):** Aluminum is added to steel as a deoxidizer. Lowest value; tolerant of contamination; common destination for low-grade scrap. Used by steel mills including Nucor, Steel Dynamics, Cleveland-Cliffs (US), and Ternium (Mexico).

A PR facility's first product is most likely **baled UBC at the can-to-can grade** (i.e., 95%+ aluminum content by weight, low Fe/Si/Cu contamination), exported to a U.S. East Coast mill. The proposal should not promise more than this; upgrading to casting-alloy or in-PR ingot is a Phase 2, not Phase 1.

## 6. Hazards and risks (called out honestly)

This section is the most important one for a realistic proposal. Each hazard is concrete, named, and cited.

### 6.1 Hurricane risk

- **Hurricane María (September 2017)** — Category 4 at landfall on the southeast coast (Yabucoa). Caused an **island-wide blackout** that lasted 3–6 months in many areas; 2.5M tons of debris; ~$90B damage. Source: USGS and FEMA reporting (summarized in ASCE 2019 <https://issuu.com/coleccionpuertorriquena/docs/2019-puerto-rico-report-card-final/s/11198408>).
- **Hurricane Fiona (September 2022)** — Category 1 at landfall in the southwest (Lajas / Ponce area); caused a **fresh island-wide blackout** within 24 hours of María. Source: <https://www.npr.org/2022/09/23/1124345084/impact-hurricane-fiona-puerto-rico> and <https://www.energy.gov/ceser/hurricane-fiona-situation-reports>. The U.S. government allocated more than $3B in hazard-mitigation funding post-María, but "few projects have gotten underway" (WaPo 2022).
- **Hurricane season:** June 1 to November 30, with peak August–September. **A facility must be designed for a 175+ mph direct-hit wind event and a 15–25 inch single-event rainfall.** The Ponce south coast is the *windward* coast for Atlantic storms, so the south-coast site is more exposed to hurricane landfall than the north coast (which is the *leeward* coast for the prevailing trades). This is a real tension between "Ponce is the right port" and "north coast is the right hurricane exposure profile."

**The facility's hurricane plan must include:**

- Hardened main building (concrete + steel, rated to current PR building code which is the 2018 IBC with PR amendments).
- Backup power (see §2.4).
- A documented **2-week "island of self-sufficiency"** capability (fuel on site, water on site, employee sheltering, food).
- A **documented facility shut-down and restart protocol** for tropical-storm warnings (4–7 day window from warning to landfall).
- Insurance that explicitly covers named-storm wind and flood.

### 6.2 Seismic risk

- **Punta Montalva Fault** is a southwest-PR fault identified by USGS as the source of the **January 7, 2020 Mw 6.4 earthquake** and the broader 2019–2023 southwest-PR seismic sequence. Source: <https://www.usgs.gov/news/featured-story/magnitude-64-earthquake-puerto-rico> and <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0040195123002196>.
- The **Muertos Trough subduction zone** (south of the island) and the **Puerto Rico Trench** (north of the island, the deepest point in the Atlantic) are both active seismic features. A repeat of the 1918-magnitude-PR earthquake is a low-probability but high-consequence event.
- **Ponce, Peñuelas, and Juana Díaz** are all in the **Punta Montalva seismic zone**. A facility sited in Ponce is in a higher-seismic zone than a facility sited in the north coast (Carolina / Bayamón) or the east coast (Caguas / Juncos / Las Piedras).
- The 2020 events destroyed or damaged several industrial buildings in the southwest and led to a long-running shutdown of the Costa Sur power plant (the largest on the island, ~990 MW), which directly worsened the LUMA/Genera PR reliability crisis.

**Seismic design must follow the 2018 PR building code seismic provisions** (which incorporate ASCE 7-16). For a heavy industrial facility with vertical storage (bales, scrap), this is non-trivial and can add 5–10% to structural costs.

### 6.3 Flood zones (FEMA)

PR participates in the **National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)** and the FEMA Flood Map Service Center publishes PR's FIRM panels. Source: <https://www.fema.gov/locations/risk-reduction/puerto%20rico> and <https://msc.fema.gov/portal>. Coastal municipalities (the entire north coast from Rincón to Fajardo, the south coast from Ponce to Guayama, the Vieques and Culebra coasts) have **Ve zones** (high-velocity wave action) or **AE zones** (1% annual chance of flooding, with base flood elevation). Inland municipalities have **AE** and **X-shaded** zones around rivers (especially the Río Grande de Arecibo, Río Bayamón, Río de la Plata, and the rivers of the central mountain spine).

**A proposed site should be in an unshaded X zone or a low-risk AE zone with finished-floor elevation above the base flood elevation + 1–2 feet of freeboard.** Avoid Ve zones entirely.

### 6.4 Energy resilience

Covered in §2.4. The 154-hour LUMA forecast for July 2024 – June 2025 is the planning baseline. A facility must have **on-site generation sized for 100% of its critical load** (sorting, baling, ventilation) for at least 7 days of continuous operation. This is a major capex line item.

### 6.5 Insurance costs

PR commercial property and casualty insurance is **materially more expensive** than the U.S. mainland. Industry sources report 2× to 4× mainland premiums for comparable coverage, driven by hurricane frequency and historical losses. The CARICOM and PR markets are small (fewer carriers writing) and re-insurance is constrained. The facility's pro-forma should budget insurance at **2.5–3.5% of insured value annually** as a planning assumption, and should obtain firm quotes from at least three PR-licensed carriers (e.g., MAPFRE, Cooperativa de Seguros Múltiples, Triple-S) before closing project finance.

### 6.6 Workforce — population decline and skilled-labor competition

- PR's population is **~3.2 million in 2024–2025**, down from a peak of **3.8 million in 2004** (a 17% decline). Source: <https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/02/05/key-findings-about-puerto-rico/> and <https://www.census.gov/quickfacts/PR>. The U.S. Census Bureau's 2023 estimate is in the same range.
- Net out-migration to the U.S. mainland has been ~30,000–80,000 per year in the post-María years (it has slowed in 2024–2025 but continues).
- The labor-force participation rate in PR is among the lowest in the U.S. system (well below 50% in some post-María years), reflecting demographic aging and out-migration of working-age adults.
- **Skilled-trade competition:** pharmaceutical manufacturers (Pfizer, AbbVie, Amgen, Lilly, and the Bristol-Myers facilities) pay $25–45/hour for industrial technicians; the construction-rebuild sector (FEMA-funded) pays $20–30/hour; a recycling facility will likely need to pay $20–30/hour to attract competent staff in a tight market.

### 6.7 Political and fiscal risk — FOMB and PROMESA

- The **Financial Oversight and Management Board for Puerto Rico (FOMB, "la junta")** was established in 2016 by the **Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act (PROMESA, Pub. L. 114-187)**. Source: <https://oversightboard.pr.gov/> and <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PROMESA>.
- The FOMB certifies PR's fiscal plans, budgets, and major contracts (over $10M). **A recycling facility's major government contracts, lease of government land, or PPAs with PREPA would all be subject to FOMB review.**
- The FOMB is **politically contentious** and has been subjected to membership turnover by presidential action. In **August 2025, President Trump fired six of the seven FOMB members**, marking the first time in the Board's history. Source: <https://ieefa.org/resources/new-puerto-rico-oversight-board-members-face-same-fundamental-challenges> and <https://www.pr51st.com/whats-happening-with-puerto-ricos-fiscal-oversight-board/>. The FOMB has not been dissolved and continues to function as of 2025–2026, but its membership stability is uncertain.
- For the proposal, this means: **plan for 12–18 months of government approval timeline** (OGPe + JCA + DDEC + potentially FOMB), and budget for legal counsel that has FOMB experience.

### 6.8 Other risks

- **Permit-process risk** — PR's *Permit Process Reform* work (the 2025 *Task Force para Simplificación del Sistema de Permiso de Puerto Rico* report, source: <https://www.desarrollo.pr.gov/en>) is ongoing; the system remains slow and inconsistent.
- **Construction-labor inflation** — post-María reconstruction has kept construction labor costs in PR 20–50% above mainland norms.
- **Logistics disruption** — Jones Act shipping from PR is essential and cannot be substituted with foreign-flag carriers for the U.S. mainland trade; this limits the facility's ability to shop the lowest-cost carrier.
- **Currency / federal-tax risk** — PR uses USD; no FX risk. Federal tax policy risk is real (Act 60's federal acceptance is not guaranteed long-term).

## 7. Synthesis and proposal implications

A realistic, government-ready proposal for an aluminum-can recycling facility in PR should be structured around the following honest conclusions:

1. **The structural tailwind is real and binding.** PR is running out of landfill space (29 landfills, mostly unlined, many over capacity), recycling rates are in the 9–14% range, and the SWA's own 2014 testimony estimated $500M/year of recoverable material being landfilled. A facility that aggregates and exports 20,000–40,000 t/yr of UBC addresses a real, documentable waste-stream problem.

2. **There is no in-PR market for the output.** The facility is an *export-from-PR* operation; the value proposition to PR is **landfill diversion and jobs**, not metal sales. The proposal should be written for a Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources (DRNA), DDEC, and FOMB audience that understands this framing.

3. **The site choice has a real trade-off.** South coast (Ponce / Peñuelas) is the right port but the wrong seismic and hurricane-exposure profile. North coast (Bayamón / Cataño / Guaynabo) is the wrong port (more expensive export via San Juan) but better seismic and grid-stability profile. East coast (Caguas / Juncos / Yabucoa) is a reasonable compromise. The proposal should present a multi-criteria site-selection analysis and identify a **single primary site and a backup site**.

4. **Capital cost must include full energy and hurricane resilience.** Plan for 8–15% of capex dedicated to on-site power, water, and structural hardening. Total capex for a 30,000 t/yr UBC export facility is in the **$25–60M range**, depending on site and scope (these are order-of-magnitude estimates; an engineering firm needs to produce firm numbers).

5. **The principal existing player is IFCO Recycling.** A proposal that ignores IFCO will not be taken seriously. A proposal that proposes partnership with IFCO is more credible. A proposal that proposes building a competing IFCO-scale plant is the most risky of the three and requires very clear differentiation (e.g., on-grade UBC vs. mixed recyclables, or in-PR decoating vs. baling).

6. **Tax-incentive structuring under Act 60-2019 is real and should be modeled, but is not automatic.** The 4% corporate rate, 90%+ property-tax exemption, and dividend-exemption features are material to project IRR. The proposal should include a tax-structuring section by a qualified PR tax counsel (e.g., McConnell Valdés, Fiddler González & Rodríguez, or Ferraiuoli LLC).

7. **The FOMB approval process is a timeline and a risk, not a "no."** Major projects subject to FOMB review are common (the LUMA contract, the Genera contract, the Peñuelas renewables project, the Roosevelt Roads redevelopment, etc.). A clean, financially-sound recycling project with a clear environmental benefit and no operating subsidy should pass FOMB review within the standard 12–18 month window.

8. **Honest data gaps to disclose in the proposal itself:**
   - Aluminum can tonnage in PR MSW: estimated, not measured.
   - Per-capita can consumption in PR: estimated from U.S. mainland patterns, not measured.
   - Post-2017 PR recycling rate: not publicly updated.
   - 2024–2025 landfill capacity: not publicly updated; "a few years" per EPA 2021.
   - Jones Act shipping cost premium for PR–mainland UBC: estimated.
   - The 154-hour LUMA outage figure: a forecast, not a measurement; a *worse* 2025–2026 figure is plausible.

9. **Recommended proposal outline:**

   - **Chapter 1 — Problem statement** (cite ASCE 2019, EPA GFX, PR SWA 2014 testimony, DRNA 2022 Plan).
   - **Chapter 2 — Market and offtake** (acknowledge the in-PR market is zero; identify the 3–4 mainland mills as offtakers).
   - **Chapter 3 — Site selection** (multi-criteria; identify 1 + 1 sites).
   - **Chapter 4 — Process and technology** (dry mechanical + baler, with a Phase 2 decoater/ingot line).
   - **Chapter 5 — Permits and regulatory compliance** (JCA, OGPe, PR OSHA, EPA Region 2, FEMA flood).
   - **Chapter 6 — Energy, water, and resilience** (LUMA, Genera, on-site generation, Act 17-2019 net metering, PRASA).
   - **Chapter 7 — Tax and financial structure** (Act 60-2019, FOMB review path, debt and equity sources).
   - **Chapter 8 — Risk register** (hurricane, seismic, flood, energy, insurance, workforce, FOMB, political).
   - **Chapter 9 — Jobs and economic impact** (target: 40–80 direct, 100–200 indirect at full operation).
   - **Chapter 10 — Schedule and milestones** (24-month construction + commissioning, 12-month OGPe/JCA pre-construction).

10. **One-sentence summary of the realistic case:**

> *PR has a real, documentable aluminum-can waste-stream problem; the facility's economic case rests on landfill-diversion payments, Act 60-2019 tax incentives, and the U.S. mainland UBC commodity market, with all offtake shipped to one of three East Coast secondary smelters; the technical and financial case is sound, the political and regulatory timeline is long, and the hurricane / seismic / energy risks are real and must be priced into both the capex budget and the operating plan.*
