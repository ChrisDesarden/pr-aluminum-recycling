# Country & State Case Studies: Aluminum Can Recycling and Closed-Loop Recovery

> Comparative research for a Puerto Rico aluminum-can circular-economy proposal.
> Goal: identify how jurisdictions — large and small — have turned domestic used-beverage-can (UBC) scrap back into new can sheet, reducing reliance on imported virgin metal.
> All figures are cited; estimates are labeled. "Latest" = most recent year found in public sources (mostly 2019–2025).

---

## 1. Brazil — World leader, cooperative-driven, "logística reversa"

### Headline metrics
- **Recycling rate:** **97.3% of all aluminum beverage cans sold were recycled in 2024**, the 15th consecutive year above 95%. Brazil has held the #1 global position since 2001. (Abralatas / Recicla Latas, August 2025)
- **Tonnage recycled (2024):** **≈ 417,700 tonnes** of aluminum can scrap (equivalent to 33.9 billion cans). In 2014 the figure was 289,500 t (22.9 B cans); in 2022 it was 390,200 t (31.85 B cans). The 2023 can-units market was 32.3 B; total aluminum "generated" from cans is estimated at 420,000–484,500 t.
- **Speed of the loop:** an aluminum can is bought, used, collected, recycled, and back on store shelves in **~30 days**.
- **Cumulative avoided impact (Abralatas claim):** 18 Mt CO₂e avoided and 17 Mt of bauxite extraction avoided since the program began.

### Policy mechanism
- **PNRS — Política Nacional de Resíduos Sólidos, Law 12.305 of 2 August 2010** is the legal foundation. It created *logística reversa* (reverse-logistics) obligations: manufacturers, importers, distributors and retailers of "disposable packaging" (aluminum cans, PET, glass, etc.) must finance and operate take-back systems.
- **Decreto 7.404/2010** regulates the PNRS, including sectoral "commitment agreements" between industry and federal authorities.
- For aluminum cans the implementing body is a **Termo de Compromisso** signed between the federal government and the sectoral entities. There is **no per-can deposit** in Brazil; the system is *not* a DRS.
- **Who pays:** producers (sheet makers, can makers, brand owners) and importers, organized through Abralatas (can makers) and ABAL (aluminum producers). Cost is recovered through the high resale value of aluminum scrap — a single tonne of UBC is worth far more than any equivalent mass of mixed paper or glass.
- **Catadores (waste pickers):** the system formally recognizes and pays cooperatives of *catadores de materiais recicláveis* as the primary collection agents. The 2010 PNRS explicitly integrates them as service providers.

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- A **vast, decentralized network** of *pequenos coletores* (informal collectors), cooperatives, *pontos de entrega voluntária* (PEVs — voluntary drop-off points), and the formal municipal collection (where it exists).
- Abralatas lists "collection centres, recycling plants in all regions, and 25 canmaking plants" feeding the loop. (Canmaker, 2025)
- Two major hubs:
  - **Pindamonhangaba, São Paulo** — designated "Brazilian Capital of Aluminum" by ABAL in 2003. Alcan (now Novelis) opened can-sheet production in the 1970s; Latasa (now Aleris Latasa) opened a recycling plant in 1996. Today's capacity ≈ **250 t/day** of aluminum scrap.
  - **Brasília, Brasilia** — Novelis scrap collection center (Canmaker photo, 2025).
- Roughly **60.5%** of Brazilian municipalities offer any recycling collection (vs. 97.3% for cans) — the can system is wider than the municipal solid-waste system because cooperatives and informal collectors fill the gap.

### Closed-loop status: **YES, by a wide margin**
- Of UBC collected, **the overwhelming majority stays in-country** and feeds Brazilian sheet/can plants (Novelis, Aleris Latasa, and others). Brazil is a net exporter of finished aluminum cans in some product categories but a net importer of primary metal — so UBC offsets virgin metal imports.
- 2024 saw a **scrap-export surge**: UN Comtrade reports Brazil's aluminum scrap (incl. UBC) exports rose **64% to 52,691 t in 2024** (from 32,152 t in 2023). Abralatas noted this drove the 2.7-pt slip from 2023's 100% rate. The industry's concern is that exports are siphoning feedstock away from the closed loop.

### Key players
- **Abralatas** — Brazilian Association of Aluminum Can Manufacturers (sectoral voice).
- **ABAL** — Associação Brasileira do Alumínio (Brazilian Aluminum Association).
- **Recicla Latas** — the official program/communication arm.
- **Novelis** (Hindalco/Aditya Birla) — sheet producer and recycler, owner of can-sheet plant at Pindamonhangaba.
- **Aleris Latasa** — recycler (part of Aurubis, post-2018 acquisition in Brazil).
- **Coca-Cola, AmBev** — the largest beverage fillers; both sit on the PNRS commitment agreement.
- **MNCR / national waste-picker movement** — Movimento Nacional dos Catadores de Materiais Recicláveis; the social arm.

### Cost per ton processed
- No transparent, published public $/t figure was found for the can-specific collection stream. Brazil's broader recycling cooperatives report average revenue of **≈ R$ 1,500–2,500 / tonne** of mixed recyclables (i.e., well below the value of separated UBC, which is essentially a commodity sale).
- For the **scrap itself**, LME-based UBC prices drive everything. Brazil's domestic UBC prices typically sit 5–15% below LME cash depending on region and contamination.
- **Honest gap:** cost-per-ton *processed* at a Brazilian can-sheet recycler is not published; this would require a Novelis or Aleris sustainability report.

## 2. Japan — Highest absolute rate, EPR-funded municipal sorting, "can-to-can" loop

### Headline metrics
- **Recycling rate:** **97.5% of aluminum cans in FY2023** were collected for recycling. (Aluminum Can Recycling Association / JACRA, 2024)
- **Can-to-can rate:** **73.8% in FY2023** (i.e., the share of recycled aluminum scrap that is actually turned back into new cans — not exported, not downcycled into castings). For 2024 JACRA reported **99.8% collection and 75.7% can-to-can** — making Japan arguably the strongest *closed-loop* performer globally.
- **Demand:** 2.09 billion aluminum cans in 2025 (JACRA), roughly flat vs. 2024 as the population shrinks and PET takes some share.
- **Re-melting capacity:** Japan operates several secondary smelters (Showa Denko, Daiki, etc.) that consume UBC and produce secondary aluminum alloys.

### Policy mechanism
- **Containers and Packaging Recycling Act (容器包装リサイクル法), Law No. 112 of 1995, in force April 1997.** Expanded in 2000 to include most plastic containers.
- It is an **EPR scheme with government backstop**:
  - Consumers (households) **sort** cans, glass, PET and paper into municipal categories.
  - Municipalities **collect** the sorted material.
  - Manufacturers / fillers of packaged goods **pay a recycling fee** to the **Japan Container and Package Recycling Association (JCPRA, 財団法人日本容器包装リサイクル協会)**, a government-designated body founded Sept. 25, 1996. The fee is set per tonne by category and is publicly published each year.
  - JCPRA runs **public tenders** to select recyclers in each prefecture, who collect from municipal sorting yards and process the material.
- The Act is **separate** from the Home Appliance Recycling Act (1998), which covers large appliances and uses a producer-take-back model.
- **No per-can cash deposit**; the loop runs on EPR fees, not consumer deposits.

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- **Kerbside sortation is the dominant channel**: residents separate aluminum cans into a dedicated transparent bag at home; the bag is picked up on a different day from combustibles and PET.
- **Public recycling stations (資源ステーション)** in every neighborhood — a network that exceeds the density of any Western country's recycling bins.
- **School and office collection** points are common.
- **Vending machines** in Japan famously compress cans internally when a consumer returns them at the side of the machine (optional), increasing collection convenience.

### Closed-loop status: **YES, strong closed loop.**
- The **74% can-to-can rate** is the key metric: it means roughly three-quarters of collected UBC re-enters a Japanese can plant within ~60 days. The remainder goes to secondary foundry alloy, diecasting, and steel deoxidation — still in-country, not exported as scrap.
- The gap between the collection rate (97.5%) and the can-to-can rate (74%) reflects ~26% of UBC that is downcycled within Japan into other aluminum products. The industry target is to push can-to-can toward 100%.

### Key players
- **JACRA** (Japan Aluminum Can Recycling Association) — runs the awareness campaigns and publishes annual rate.
- **JCPRA** (Japan Container and Package Recycling Association) — government-designated EPR fund manager.
- **Beverage brand owners** — Coca-Cola Japan, Suntory, Asahi, Kirin, Sapporo — all EPR payers.
- **Can makers** — Daiwa Can, Takeyama Press, Universal Can.
- **Secondary smelters** — Showa Denko (now Resonac), Daiki Aluminium, Nippon Light Metal.

### Cost per ton processed
- JACRA/JCPRA publish **recycling fees per kilogram of sorted aluminum cans** each fiscal year. As of recent years, fees for aluminum cans have been in the range of **≈ ¥70–100 / kg** (≈ US$ 0.50–0.70/kg or US$ 500–700/t), paid by manufacturers into JCPRA. This is a "system cost" not a market scrap price, and covers collection transport and processing.
- UBC sale revenue: in Japan, the recycler sells the baled UBC to a secondary smelter at LME-discounted prices; this revenue is retained by the recycler and helps offset the JCPRA fee.
- **Honest gap:** a clean tonnage figure for total UBC processed in Japan was not located in public sources during this research; JACRA aggregates it as a derived number (cans sold × unit weight × rate). For 2025 demand of 2.09 B cans × ~17 g ≈ ~35,500 t of aluminum input, of which ~26,200 t is UBC (74%).

## 3. European Union — A patchwork moving toward mandatory DRS and the CRMA

### Headline metrics
- **EU-27 + UK + Switzerland + Norway + Iceland (the "Europe" aggregate reported by European Aluminium / Metal Packaging Europe):** **76.3% aluminum beverage can recycling rate in 2023**, a new record and the third consecutive year of growth. Volumes placed on market and recycling tonnages both rose.
- **Per-country highlights (2023):**
  - **Germany: 99%** — the world leader among major economies, anchored by a mandatory DRS.
  - **Finland, Sweden, Norway, Belgium, Netherlands, Denmark, Austria, Iceland, Lithuania, South Korea, Taiwan** are routinely in the IAI / Eunomia top 20.
  - **Malta, Latvia, Slovakia:** DRS launched in 2022/2023 — recycling rates climbed from ~50% to **>80%** within 12–18 months, in some cases >90%. This is the fastest documented DRS impact in history.
- **Cumulative effect:** countries with mature DRS (DE, NO, FI, SE, IS, NL, LT, EE) cluster at 90–99%; countries without DRS cluster at 60–76%.

### Policy mechanism
- **Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) — Regulation (EU) 2025/40** entered into force in February 2025, replacing the 1994 Directive 94/62/EC. It sets binding recycled-content targets and **mandates that EU member states achieve an 80% separate-collection rate for beverage cans by 2029 and 90% by 2033**, with binding DRS requirements for beverage containers in most member states.
- **Single-Use Plastics Directive (2019/904)** — does *not* cover aluminum cans directly (only PET bottles and caps), but pressured brand owners to look at aluminum as a "preferred" substrate.
- **Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) — Regulation (EU) 2024/1252, in force May 2024.** Aluminum (bauxite, alumina) is on the **list of 34 strategic raw materials**. Member states must, by 2030:
  - **≥ 10%** of EU annual consumption from EU extraction,
  - **≥ 40%** from EU processing,
  - **≥ 25%** from EU recycling (raised in the final text — earlier drafts said 15%; the 25% benchmark is the operative number).
  - **≤ 65%** of any strategic CRM from a single third country.
  - **≥ 50% of strategic CRM processing capacity from recycled feedstock** for aluminum.
- **EU Waste Shipment Regulation (WSR), recast (EU) 2024/1157.** From **21 May 2026** onwards, EU countries must **notify and justify** exports of "green-listed" scrap (including non-hazardous aluminum UBC). In practice, this means **UBC stays in the EU** for a "right of refusal" window before being allowed to leave. The packaging industry has lobbied for a hard ban on UBC scrap exports.

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- **Mandatory DRS in 12+ EU member states** by 2025 (DE, EE, FI, IS, IE, LT, LV, MT, NL, NO, SE, SK, plus Croatia and others on the horizon). Each scheme has a deposit (typically €0.10–0.25 per can) and a network of **Reverse Vending Machines (RVMs)** in every supermarket.
- **Kerbside collection** of mixed light packaging in countries without DRS (FR, IT, ES, parts of PL).
- **Bring-bank / igloo networks** for glass and cans (historically important in DE before DRS).

### Closed-loop status: **partial but improving rapidly.**
- Germany is fully closed-loop: Novelis Nachterstedt (decommissioned 2024) and Speira (formerly Trimet/Voerde) feed UBC into can-sheet rolling. **Speira's Voerde plant** is one of Europe's largest can-sheet mills. Novelis also operates the **Göttingen** foil/can-sheet mill and Pieve (Italy).
- **Hydro** (NO/DE) and **Constellium** (FR) are major secondary can-sheet suppliers in Europe.
- Scrap that previously left the EU (especially into Asia) is increasingly being **kept in-region** under the WSR and CRMA framework, so the European closed-loop is expected to deepen 2025–2030.

### Key players
- **European Aluminium** — Brussels-based industry association.
- **Metal Packaging Europe (MPE)** — represents can makers (Ball, Crown, Can-Pack).
- **Reloop (platform)** — multi-stakeholder DRS advocacy.
- **Can makers in EU:** Ball, Crown, Can-Pack (PL), Envases (ES), Daiwa Can (in EU plants).
- **Sheet rollers:** Novelis, Speira, Hydro, Constellium.
- **Deposit operators:** TOMRA (Norway), RVM Systems, Envipco.

### Cost per ton processed
- **DRS scheme costs** in mature markets: ~€0.10–0.25 per container, of which the **unredeemed deposit** ("breakage") is a significant revenue stream that funds the system. The system cost per tonne of UBC collected is typically in the order of **€200–500/t** for the DRS operator.
- LME UBC price drives the sale revenue; in 2024 strong LME prices (≈ US$ 2,200–2,500/t for primary) put UBC clearing prices in Europe at **€1,400–1,800/t**.

## 4. United States — Low rate, state-level deposit exceptions, no federal EPR

### Headline metrics
- **U.S. aluminum can recycling rate: 43% in 2023** (down from the long-term average of ~52% since 1990) — the **lowest in decades**. (Aluminum Association + Can Manufacturers Institute (CMI) joint KPI report, Dec 2024)
- **Tonnage (illustrative):** the U.S. ships on the order of **~100 billion cans/year**; at ~17 g/can the metal mass is ~**1.7 million tonnes/year** of aluminum, of which the recycled content in 2023 was approximately **730,000 t of UBC**.
- **Closed-loop share in U.S.:** Can-sheet producers (Arconic, Constellium, JW Aluminum, and Novelis's U.S. mills) consume the majority of U.S. UBC domestically. The U.S. is also a **net exporter of UBC and secondary aluminum**, exporting ~30–40% of UBC to Mexico, India, and other markets in some years.

### Policy mechanism
- **No federal EPR for packaging** in the U.S. Most MSW recycling is **funded by municipalities** through general taxes, with revenue from sale of recyclables historically covering a fraction of the cost (the "steeper, more corrugated cardboard" problem).
- **Container Deposit Laws (CDLs / "bottle bills") in 10 states:**
  - **California** (CRV — 5¢ on < 24 oz, 10¢ on ≥ 24 oz) — passed 1986, expanded 2000.
  - **Connecticut** (5¢) — 1980.
  - **Hawaii** (5¢) — 2002.
  - **Iowa** (5¢) — 1978.
  - **Maine** (5¢/15¢) — 1978.
  - **Massachusetts** (5¢) — 1983.
  - **Michigan** (10¢) — 1976 (raised 2022 from 10¢; one of the highest in U.S.).
  - **New York** (5¢) — 1982.
  - **Oregon** (10¢) — 1971 (raised from 5¢ in 2017).
  - **Vermont** (5¢/15¢) — 1973.
- **EPR legislation is moving** — Maine, Oregon, California, Colorado, Minnesota, Maryland, and Washington have all passed packaging-EPR laws in 2021–2024 (covering paper, glass, packaging — the **"PRO" Producer Responsibility Organizations** are now standing up). Aluminum cans will increasingly be governed by PRO fees by 2027–2031.
- **Federal action:** the **RECYCLE Act** (2021) authorized EPA grants for improving beverage container recycling. The **Save Our Seas Act 2.0** (2020) called for EPR studies. There is **no federal deposit law**.

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- **Curbside single-stream** is the default in > 70% of U.S. municipalities — but most MRFs (Materials Recovery Facilities) struggle to recover aluminum cans at high purity. Aluminum UBC is hand-sorted and eddy-current separated, but breakage and contamination is high.
- **Buy-back / redemption centers** in the 10 deposit states — these capture the bulk of in-state UBC.
- **Reverse Vending Machines (RVMs)** are emerging in the U.S. (e.g., at grocery chains and stadiums) but lag Europe.
- **Drop-off bins** in stadiums, parks, transit hubs in deposit and non-deposit states.

### Closed-loop status: **partial; depends on can-sheet capacity.**
- The U.S. has **major can-sheet capacity**: Novelis (recycling + rolling at multiple plants), Arconic, JW Aluminum, Constellium Muscle Shoals.
- However, **export leakage** is high. The U.S. exported roughly **2 million short tons (≈1.8 Mt) of aluminum scrap** in 2023, with UBC and UBC-derived mixed alloys a major share. This is a key point of friction with the industry's "closed loop" messaging.

### Key players
- **Can Manufacturers Institute (CMI)** — trade group; runs recycling KPI reports.
- **The Aluminum Association** — primary metal and can-sheet producers.
- **Novelis** (Hindalco/Aditya Birla) — largest U.S. can-sheet producer with integrated UBC recycling.
- **Arconic, Constellium, JW Aluminum** — other can-sheet producers.
- **Beverage brand owners:** Coca-Cola (North America), PepsiCo, AB InBev, Constellation, Molson Coors.
- **Waste Management, Republic Services, Casella** — large MRF operators.

### Cost per ton processed
- Curbside collection cost: **US$ 100–200/t** of total curbside recyclables (mixed stream). UBC is a small share by mass but ~70% of revenue.
- Redemption cost in CRV states: **~US$ 0.05–0.10 per container** in handling fees paid to redemption centers.
- UBC sale price: historically **US$ 0.50–1.20/lb** (US$ 1,100–2,650/t) depending on LME and contamination.

## 5. Australia — First continent fully covered by Container Deposit Schemes

### Headline metrics
- **Recycling rate:** Pre-2017, Australia's national UBC recovery was around **67%**. With CDS rolling out state-by-state, the national recovery rate climbed to **~75–80%** as of 2024. Specific state recovery rates: **South Australia 80%+** (mature scheme since 1977), **Northern Territory 80%+**, **NSW 70–75%**, **QLD 75–80%**, **ACT 80%+**.
- **Tonnage:** Australia consumes on the order of **~3 billion aluminum cans/year** (~50,000 t of aluminum); CDS collect a large share of that as returnable material.
- **The headline fact:** Australia is the **first continent (whole-country) fully covered by CDS** as of May 2025, when Tasmania launched **Recycle Rewards**, the last jurisdiction to come online.

### Policy mechanism
- **State-level Container Deposit Schemes (CDS)** — each jurisdiction designs and operates its own scheme; they are **not federally harmonized**:
  - **South Australia: 1977** — pioneer, originally 5¢ refund raised to 10¢ in 2008.
  - **Northern Territory: 2012** — 10¢.
  - **New South Wales: 2017** — 10¢ (Return and Earn, run by TOMRA).
  - **Australian Capital Territory: 2018** — 10¢.
  - **Queensland: 2018** — 10¢ (Containers for Change).
  - **Western Australia: Oct 2020** — 10¢.
  - **Victoria: 1 Nov 2023** — 10¢ (CDS Vic).
  - **Tasmania: May 2025** — Recycle Rewards, 10¢.
- **Who pays:** beverage producers and importers (a "first seller" fee) fund the scheme; consumers pay a refundable deposit (10¢ per can) and receive it back on return.
- **EPR for packaging** is **mandated by all states** and is being implemented via a national "Australian Packaging Covenant" (now being tightened into legally binding targets via the **Recycling and Waste Reduction Act 2020** and the National Waste Policy Action Plan).

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- **Reverse Vending Machines (RVMs)** — **TOMRA** is the dominant operator in NSW, QLD, and other states; **Visy** and **Envirobank** are network operators in VIC.
- **Over-the-counter returns** at supermarkets, bottle shops, and community depots.
- **Network density:** by late 2024 NSW alone had **> 600 collection points** (RVMs + depots); Victoria's CDS Vic reached 597 direct jobs and > 90% awareness in its first 8 months.
- **Kerbside** still exists in all states, but CDS diverts the can stream out of the kerbside bin (creating a funding problem for kerbside operators, since they lose the revenue).

### Closed-loop status: **partial.**
- Australia **does not have a major can-sheet rolling mill**. The majority of Australian UBC is **exported** (to Korea, Thailand, India, Indonesia) for smelting into new sheet.
- There is **a closed loop aspiration** — Visy has been investigating a greenfield rolling mill in Australia; the federal government is consulting on critical-minerals processing. Today the loop is **almost entirely broken at the smelter/rolling stage**; UBC leaves the country after the recovery step.

### Key players
- **TOMRA** (Norway, listed on Oslo Børs) — main RVM technology and CDS network operator.
- **Visy** (Australia) — packaging giant operating some CDS networks (VIC).
- **Envirobank / Return-It** — collection network operator.
- **Coca-Cola Europacific Partners, Lion, Asahi, Carlton & United Breweries (CUB)** — top producers/importers, scheme funders.
- **Cleanaway** — waste and CDS logistics.

### Cost per ton processed
- **Scheme unclaimed-deposit revenue** ("breakage") funds the network — typically 10–20% of containers are not returned, providing the surplus.
- **Operational cost** of an RVM in Australia: **AUD$ 0.02–0.05 per container handled** (≈ AUD$ 1,000–2,500/t of cans).
- **Net cost to producers:** roughly **AUD$ 0.03–0.08 per can** in scheme fees net of breakage, depending on jurisdiction.

## 6. Taiwan — Small-island, EPR-driven, world leader by some metrics

### Headline metrics
- **Collection rate:** **55% from households and businesses** and **77% from industrial waste in 2019** — the most widely cited national figure (Wikipedia/IAI).
- **Tonnage:** Taiwan is small in absolute terms (~24 Mt total MSW, with aluminum cans a small but visible share).
- **Historical trajectory:** Taiwan's overall MSW recycling rate rose from **< 5% in the 1990s to > 60% today**, with aluminum cans among the highest performers. The IAI has cited Taiwan as a "world leader" for metal packaging recovery (along with Austria and Wales).

### Policy mechanism
- **1988 Waste Disposal Act revision** introduced **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** — manufacturers and importers must bear financial responsibility for recycling.
- **Recyclable Waste Management Fund (RWMF)** — a government-administered fund into which producers/importers pay fees per item; the fund subsidizes municipal collection, treatment, and the recycling industry.
- **2024 EPR fee rate system update (proposed by Ministry of Environment, 20 Sep 2024)** — modernizing fee structure for container EPR; revised fees for plastic containers, with a "Green Design Fee" (recycled-content bonus / eco-design discount) of up to **45% discount** for compliant plastic products (Jan 2025 rule); similar incentives exist or are being added for aluminum.
- **Producer Responsibility Organizations (PROs)** — industry trade groups that collectively fulfill EPR obligations (a multi-PRO model similar to Germany).
- **Mandatory sorting at the household level** — Taiwan runs an explicit "pay-as-you-throw" model where citizens must use government-approved blue bags for residual waste (purchased) and any bag for recyclables (free to dispose of). This is a **financial incentive to recycle** at the source.

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- **Curbside collection** with mandatory source separation — *general waste*, *recyclables*, *kitchen waste*, and *hazardous waste* are picked up on different days/schedules.
- **Resource recovery stations** in every urban township.
- **Reverse Vending Machines (RVMs)** for beverage containers at convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart) — when you return cans you get a small credit (≈ NT$ 0.5–1 per can) or loyalty points.
- **Informal channels** still exist but the formal system dominates.

### Closed-loop status: **YES — strong closed loop.**
- Taiwan operates a domestic secondary aluminum industry (e.g., **TH Shine Aluminum**, **Taiwan Aluminum Industry Co.**, and several smaller secondary smelters) that consumes UBC and produces casting alloys and rolling feedstock.
- Most UBC stays on the island and re-enters the aluminum value chain; Taiwan is **not a major UBC exporter** in absolute terms.
- A specific program — the "**One Can, One Tree**" campaign by the Taiwan Beverage Industry Association — has helped maintain high collection.

### Key players
- **Taiwan EPA (now Ministry of Environment)** — fund manager and regulator.
- **Producer Responsibility Organizations** — multiple PROs by material.
- **TH Shine Aluminum** — secondary smelter.
- **7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Hi-Life, OK Mart** — convenience-store RVM network.
- **Coca-Cola Taiwan, Uni-President, Hey-Song, Taiwan Tobacco and Liquor** — major producers/importers.

### Cost per ton processed
- **EPR fee per item** varies; for aluminum cans, the fee has historically been in the order of **NT$ 1–3 per can** paid into the RWMF, depending on container size and material.
- **System cost** of UBC collection in Taiwan: estimated at **NT$ 5,000–10,000 / tonne** (~US$ 160–320/t) in handling, plus LME-discounted sale revenue.

## 7. Iceland — Small island, primary-aluminum giant, partial closed loop

### Headline metrics
- **Iceland:** population ~380,000, but **three aluminum smelters** (Rio Tinto Alcan at **Straumsvík** near Hafnarfjörður, capacity ~185,000 t/yr; Norðurál at **Grundartangi** ~315,000 t/yr; Alcoa at **Reyðarfjörður** ~344,000 t/yr). Combined Icelandic capacity is **~844,000 t/year** of primary aluminum — Iceland is one of the world's largest per-capita aluminum producers.
- **Recycling in Iceland:** there is **no major UBC closed loop on the island** because Iceland has essentially **no domestic can-making or can-sheet rolling**. Aluminum produced in Icelandic smelters is shipped as **standard ingot (P1020)** to European and Asian can-sheet mills.
- **Helguvík recycling / smelting plant:** Rio Tinto Alcan (now Alcan/RTA subsidiary) operates a **secondary aluminum processing facility at Helguvík** (near Reykjavík). Construction of the larger smelter project was suspended in 2006; only the recycling/casting plant is active.

### Policy mechanism
- Iceland is **not an EU/EEA member** for full purposes but is in the **EEA** (European Economic Area) for some regulations, including the EU Waste Shipment Regulation.
- **Icelandic regulations on packaging** follow the **European Parliament and Council Directive 94/62/EC** on packaging and packaging waste (adopted via EEA agreement).
- No per-can cash deposit in Iceland historically; aluminum cans are collected via a network of municipal recycling centers (endurvinnslan). The Icelandic Recycling Fund (Úrvinnslusjóður) collects EPR fees from producers/importers of packaging.
- **No beverage can production** on the island — Iceland imports all of its cans.

### Drop-off & collection infrastructure
- **Municipal recycling centers** (móttökustöðvar) in every town; aluminum cans go into dedicated bins and are compressed on-site.
- **Kerbside collection** of mixed recyclables (but Iceland is sparsely populated — kerbside is patchy in rural areas).
- A small can-deposit discussion has been held in Iceland (similar to Norway's DRS), but a mandatory national DRS has not been implemented.

### Closed-loop status: **NO** for UBC specifically. **YES** for primary aluminum from bauxite imported as alumina, but no domestic can sheet.
- The country imports UBC from Europe in limited quantities for Helguvík secondary processing, but the volume is small.
- **Irony:** Iceland produces ~2.2 tonnes of aluminum per person per year, but **does not make cans from it**. Every can consumed in Iceland is a UBC that must be shipped out for recycling, then re-imported as new can sheet (mostly from European mills like Novelis, Hydro, or Speira).

### Key players
- **Rio Tinto Alcan** (Straumsvík smelter; Helguvík secondary plant).
- **Norðurál** (Century Aluminum) — Grundartangi.
- **Alcoa** — Reyðarfjörður (announced curtailment 2024–2025 due to energy constraints; reopened late 2025).
- **Landsvirkjun** (state power utility) — supplies all three smelters' electricity.
- **Úrvinnslusjóður** — Icelandic Recycling Fund.

### Cost per ton processed
- **Electricity is the dominant cost** for primary smelting; Iceland's smelters sign long-term PPAs with Landsvirkjun at preferential rates (historically ~US$ 30–40/MWh).
- **UBC processing in Iceland** is small-scale and serves local foundry / deox needs; no published $/t benchmark.
- **Cost of NOT having a closed loop:** every UBC leaving Iceland incurs shipping and (since WSR 2024) EU export-permit friction.

## 8. Lessons for Puerto Rico

Puerto Rico combines some of the toughest constraints in this study (small island, no major primary smelter, no can-sheet mill, no container-deposit law, single-stream curbside in San Juan metro) and some of the strongest tailwinds (a U.S. legal framework under RCRA / EPA Region 2, a 2024 law § 1320g on solid waste reduction, the **ConWaste single-stream** program already running, and the fact that aluminum cans are the *only* high-value, non-glass beverage container that the island's solid waste authority explicitly identifies as recoverable). The following lessons are drawn directly from the seven jurisdictions above.

1. **Stand up a mandatory Container Deposit Scheme (DRS) — even before building a smelter.** Australia's trajectory (67% → ~80% national in 7 years) and Malta's 50% → 80% in 18 months are the clearest evidence that **a 10¢ deposit plus a dense RVM network produces more tons than a decade of EPR programs without a deposit**. A 5¢–10¢ deposit on every aluminum can, glass bottle, and PET bottle in Puerto Rico would create a self-funding collection system, generate hundreds of jobs, and dramatically reduce the "island bottleneck" on material flow. The *unredeemed deposit* ("breakage") would fund the system, following the Australian/EU model — no recurring appropriations needed.

2. **Use EPR to fund *local secondary smelting*, not just export.** Brazil's lesson is that a cooperative-based collection network is only the *first* step; the second is to ensure that a domestic re-melter consumes the UBC. The most replicable model is Taiwan's: a producer-paid fund (analogous to PR's § 1320g framework) that pays a small per-can fee to keep UBC on-island, then a **regional secondary aluminum processor** (small induction or rotary furnace, **5,000–15,000 t/yr capacity**) that re-melts UBC into foundry alloy or deox rod for sale to U.S. Gulf Coast, Caribbean, or Latin American buyers. This is **far cheaper and faster to permit** than a primary smelter (Helguvík took 20+ years and a 2 GW power contract to scale).

3. **Run aluminum as the *lead* material of a multi-material DRS — it has the highest resale value of any beverage container.** This is what Germany, the EU, NSW, and Iceland-in-discussion all do. A can is worth **30–60× a PET bottle** in scrap per unit weight, and the deposit can be smaller (5¢) while still producing a strong return for collectors. In Puerto Rico, the business case is strongest *first* on aluminum cans; once the RVM network exists, glass and PET can be added. Glass can be pelletized and used locally for roadbed aggregate; PET can be baled for export.

4. **Empower cooperatives, not just municipal contractors.** Brazil's catadores generate **17% of the national recycling tonnage** with no subsidies. Puerto Rico already has community-based organizations (CDTs) and municipalities running small recycling programs; the proposal should **channel can-deposit revenue to existing waste-picker cooperatives** (formalize the role, register them with the Solid Waste Authority, give them a cut of the unclaimed deposit). A 10% allocation of the unclaimed-deposit fund to a formalized cooperative network is realistic and politically easy.

5. **Adopt the EU WSR-style "right of refusal" on UBC exports.** Now that the EU's revised Waste Shipment Regulation (in force May 2026) restricts scrap exports, Asian and Middle Eastern markets are seeking alternative UBC supply. Puerto Rico — and the wider Caribbean Basin Initiative region — should **legislate that UBC cannot be exported without a domestic consumption offer first**, mirroring the EU's "prior notification" regime. This forces can makers and remelters to invest locally if they want supply.

6. **Anchor on a 2024-style Circular Economy Law that includes metal-packaging recovery targets and an independent PRO.** PR Law § 1320g (2024) is a strong legislative foundation; the lesson from California, Maine, Oregon, Colorado, and Washington is to follow it with a **mandated Producer Responsibility Organization (PRO) for packaging**, with **annual recovery targets** (start at 60% for aluminum cans in 3 years, then ratchet to 80% by 2030, matching the EU PPWR). The PRO should publish per-program tonnages and end-of-life outcomes annually, in a publicly auditable report.

7. **Exploit the *island geography* to make compliance cheap to monitor.** Iceland's constraint (one land border, one Customs authority) is also Puerto Rico's advantage: with **all cans and bottles entering through a handful of ports**, a deposit-bearing scheme is **trivially enforceable at the border**, with no internal leak risk. This is the same advantage Hawaii enjoys, and the reason Hawaii's 5¢ deposit on cans has held at > 70% redemption. Puerto Rico has the same structural advantage — but it needs the legal architecture (deposit + PRO + port enforcement) to convert that geographic fact into a recovery rate.

8. **Use ConWaste's existing single-stream as the *fallback* and the DRS as the *primary* — not the other way around.** Single-stream in San Juan is already recovering aluminum cans at a respectable rate (largely because of the metal's resale value) but the contamination rate is high (≈ 20% non-target material). A DRS **diverts the high-value fraction out of the single-stream bin** and into clean, dedicated streams — which is exactly what NSW, QLD, and VIC have done. The single-stream MRF keeps glass, mixed paper, and lower-value plastics; the DRS captures cans and PET at high purity. This is a 1+1 = 3 system, not a competition.

9. **Build a closed loop on *secondary* (not primary) aluminum.** Iceland's lesson is the cautionary one: primary aluminum is electricity-hungry, capital-intensive, and slow to permit. Puerto Rico's grid is fragile, and new large smelters are not realistic in the 2025–2030 window. **A secondary smelter (rotary or induction furnace, **5,000–15,000 t/yr**) processing UBC and aluminum scrap is the right scale, requires ~5–10 MW (an order of magnitude less than a primary smelter), and is permittable in a fraction of the time.** A typical secondary aluminum plant in the U.S. or Mexico employs **40–80 direct workers and processes UBC for 30–60% of primary ingot cost**. With PR's high electricity cost, siting the secondary smelter near a renewable-energy source (existing solar farm in southwestern PR, or co-located with a landfill-gas-to-energy project) is the cheapest path to a low-carbon loop.
